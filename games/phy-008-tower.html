PHY — Physics Prototype #008 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Drop blocks to build the tallest tower you can! Each block swings back and forth — time your drop to align it with the block below.

  • Space or Click — Drop block
  • R — Restart game
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Precision: Any overhanging part breaks off, making the next block narrower.
  • Perfect drops: Land exactly on the previous block for bonus points and no shrinkage!
  • Speed: The swing gets faster as the tower grows — stay focused.
  • Miss entirely: If there is no overlap at all, your tower collapses and the game ends.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Jenga (1983, Leslie Scott) → Tetris stacking (1984) → Mobile stacking games → World’s Biggest (2012)

Leslie Scott created Jenga in 1983, inspired by a family game played in Takoradi, Ghana. The name comes from the Swahili word kujenga, meaning “to build.” While Jenga removes blocks from below, the tower-stacking archetype reverses this: drop from above, building upward. The mechanic is ancient — children stack blocks instinctively — but the game design tension comes from the shrinking platform that punishes imprecision exponentially.

Mobile games like “Stack” and “The Tower” popularized the drop-and-stack formula, proving that one-button mechanics can create compelling games when paired with physics-based consequences.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 6 — Deep Learning (2012–present)

Contemporary AI: Deep reinforcement learning, computer vision for robotic manipulation, physics simulation engines (MuJoCo, PyBullet).

Hidden Intelligence: Tower stacking is a structural engineering problem in disguise. The center of mass must remain over the support base, or the structure topples. Each imperfect placement narrows the effective platform, raising difficulty exponentially. This is the same problem MIT’s robot block-stacking research tackles: predict structural stability from visual observation, then act precisely.

The Convergence: Modern robotics uses deep learning to stack physical blocks — predicting stability, planning grasp points, and compensating for real-world physics. The game’s core loop (observe platform width, time the drop, accept the consequence) mirrors the sense-plan-act cycle of autonomous manipulation systems.

Classification

Prototype Notes

One-button physics stacking. Blocks swing horizontally and drop on input. Overhang detection slices the block, with the excess falling away. Perfect alignment detection rewards precision. Camera scrolls upward as tower grows. Sky gradient shifts with altitude. Increasing swing speed creates natural difficulty curve.
