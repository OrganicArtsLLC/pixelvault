MAZ-002 · ROAD CROSSING

MAZ — Maze Chase Prototype #002 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Guide the frog from the bottom to one of five home slots at the top. Cross the road (avoid cars) and ride logs/turtles across the river. Fill all 5 homes to clear the level.

  • ↑ ↓ ← → or WASD — Hop one tile
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Timing: Watch the rhythm of each lane before hopping.
  • River: You must land on a log or turtle — water is fatal.
  • Logs carry you: Once on a log, you drift with it. Don't drift off-screen.
  • Timer: Each crossing has a time limit. Move decisively.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Frogger (1981, Konami) → Crossy Road (2014, Hipster Whale)

Frogger (1981) by Konami asked: what if the obstacle IS the path? No maze walls, no pursuers — just rhythmic lanes of traffic and flowing water. Each lane is an independent timing puzzle. The genius is layering: road lanes move at different speeds, river objects flow in alternating directions, and the player must synchronize across all of them simultaneously.

Frogger is one of the most-ported games in history and a cultural icon. Crossy Road (2014) proved the mechanic is timeless by modernizing it for mobile with procedural generation.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (1978–1983)

Contemporary AI: Rule-based expert systems. No temporal reasoning. AI could search trees but not model continuous flows.

Hidden Intelligence: Frogger is a rhythm-matching problem disguised as a maze. Each lane is a periodic timing window. The player solves multiple independent oscillators simultaneously — a constraint satisfaction problem across time. Modern RL agents find Frogger exceptionally difficult because the reward is sparse (only awarded at the top) and requires long-horizon planning across independent timers.

The Convergence: Frogger remains a benchmark for RL difficulty. DeepMind's DQN struggles with it compared to simpler Atari games. The sparse reward + temporal coordination is exactly what makes multi-step planning hard for AI.

If AI Had Designed This: Procedurally varied lane speeds to maintain optimal difficulty — the game would tune itself to each player's rhythm perception.

Classification

Series: MAZ — Maze Chase

Frogger extends the maze series into open-field traversal. No walls — the obstacles themselves form a dynamic, rhythmic maze. The constraint isn't space, it's time.

Core Mechanic

Grid traversal through moving lanes. Discrete tile-based movement through continuous hazard streams. Road section (avoid) vs river section (ride). Five home slots create sub-goals.

What This Prototype Tests

  • Lane rhythm — does each lane feel like a distinct timing puzzle?
  • Log riding — does drifting feel natural and urgent?
  • Two-zone design — does the road→river transition shift the player's mental model?
  • Home slots — does filling all 5 create satisfying progression?
