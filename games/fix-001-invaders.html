FIX — Fixed Position Prototype #001 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Destroy all 55 aliens before they reach the ground. Take cover behind destructible shelters. Watch for the UFO bonus.

  • ← → or A D — Move ship
  • Space — Shoot (one bullet at a time, classic-style)
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Acceleration: Aliens speed up as you kill them. The last few move very fast.
  • Top rows score more: Squids (top) = 30pts, Crabs = 20pts, Octopi (bottom) = 10pts
  • Shelters: Both your bullets and alien bullets destroy shelter pixels. Use them wisely.
  • UFO: Random bonus ship at the top. Worth 50-300pts.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Taito Space Invaders (1978) → Galaxian (1979) → Galaga (1981)

Space Invaders (1978) caused a coin shortage in Japan. It invented the concept of a formation that speeds up as it shrinks — natural difficulty scaling through emergent tempo. As you kill invaders, the remaining ones march faster. The last alien is terrifyingly quick.

This escalating tempo is the actual design genius. It created tension through game mechanics, not scripted difficulty. Every tower defense, bullet hell, and wave-based game inherits this insight.

The destructible shelters add tactical depth: they protect you now but degrade over time, creating a slowly worsening defense.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (1978–1983)

Contemporary AI: Expert systems commercializing. Hopfield networks emerging. Backpropagation being rediscovered. AI could search and classify but not learn in real-time.

Hidden Intelligence: The speedup-as-aliens-die mechanic is emergent curriculum learning — the game automatically escalates difficulty as the player succeeds. AI researchers wouldn't formalize curriculum learning until ~2015. Taito's accidental performance bug was 37 years ahead of the field.

The Convergence: Space Invaders was one of the original DQN benchmarks (DeepMind, 2013). AI learned to play this exact game from raw pixels, discovering optimal strategies through the same process Taito used accidentally: trial, error, and selection pressure.

If AI Had Designed This: Intentional difficulty curves instead of accidental ones — finding the speedup parameter through simulation rather than discovering it was a bug too late to fix.

Classification

Series: FIX — Fixed Position

Games where the player holds a position (usually at the bottom) and must suppress approaching threats. The player's movement is constrained (horizontal only). The tension is temporal: can I eliminate them before they reach me?

Core Mechanic

Formation suppression with escalating tempo. 5×11 grid of aliens marches left-right-down. One player bullet allowed on screen at a time (classic constraint). Speed inversely proportional to surviving aliens.

What This Prototype Tests

  • Tempo escalation (does the speedup feel fair or cheap?)
  • One-bullet constraint (creates aim pressure — each shot matters)
  • Pixel-based shelter destruction (satisfying degradation?)
  • Formation personality (3 alien types with distinct pixel sprites)

Prototype Notes

This is a sketch. The three alien types use hand-drawn 8×6 pixel sprites with 2-frame animation, mimicking the original's distinctive marching look.
