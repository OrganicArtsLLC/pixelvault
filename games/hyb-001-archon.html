How to Play

Board Phase

Light and Dark armies face off on a 9×9 board. Players take turns moving one piece.

  • Arrow keys — Move cursor on the board
  • Space — Select a piece, then move to a destination
  • Move to an empty square to reposition; move onto an enemy to initiate combat
  • Each piece can move a set number of squares (shown when selected)

Combat Phase

When two pieces contest a square, they enter a real-time arena and fight to the death.

  • Arrow keys — Move your piece around the arena
  • Space — Fire projectile / melee attack (cooldown-based)
  • Each piece type has unique speed, HP, and attack style
  • The winner returns to the board; the loser is eliminated

Power Squares

  • 5 special squares cycle between light and dark over time
  • Pieces on a power square matching their alignment get an HP bonus in combat
  • Occupy all 5 power squares to win instantly

Victory

  • Eliminate all enemy pieces, OR
  • Occupy all 5 power squares simultaneously

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Archon: The Light and the Dark (1983, Free Fall Associates/EA) → Battle Chess (1988, Interplay)

Archon was created by Jon Freeman and Anne Westfall of Free Fall Associates, published by Electronic Arts in 1983 — one of EA's earliest titles. It asked a radical question: what if chess captures weren't automatic? What if the attacking piece had to actually fight for the square?

The result was a genre hybrid that required both strategic thinking (board positioning, piece management) and action skill (arena combat reflexes). The light/dark cycling power squares added a temporal dimension to strategy — a square that favors you now might betray you in three turns.

Battle Chess (1988) took a different approach to animated captures, but Archon's true legacy lives in games like Fire Emblem, XCOM, and any strategy game where unit encounters resolve through skill-based sub-games rather than dice rolls.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (hybrid strategic and tactical AI)

Dual Cognition: Archon requires two fundamentally different AI competencies in one game. The board phase demands strategic evaluation — piece value, positional advantage, power square control — similar to chess engines. The combat phase demands reactive control — dodging, aiming, timing — similar to action game AI.

Hierarchical Architecture: This dual-reasoning structure prefigures modern hierarchical AI systems that combine high-level strategic planners with low-level reactive controllers. A robot that plans a path across a room (strategy) while avoiding unexpected obstacles (tactics) uses the same architecture Archon demands.

If AI Had Designed This: It might have made the combat outcomes partially influence future board evaluations — a piece that barely survived combat should be treated differently than a fresh one. Modern AI would add damage persistence as an information channel.

Classification

Series: HYB — Hybrid

Games that combine two or more distinct gameplay systems into a unified experience. Archon is the founding example: strategy board + action arena.

Core Mechanic

Chess-like board strategy with real-time arena combat for captures. Two complete game systems joined at the point of conflict. Every capture is earned, not given.

What This Prototype Tests

  • Phase transitions — does switching between board and arena feel natural?
  • Piece differentiation — do different piece types feel distinct in combat?
  • Power square dynamics — does the cycling mechanic create strategic tension?
  • AI dual-competency — can a simple AI be fun in both phases?

Prototype Notes

This is a sketch rated 4/5. The 9×9 board with 8 pieces per side captures the core Archon experience. Piece types are differentiated by speed, HP, attack range, and cooldown. The AI uses random valid moves on the board and simple chase/shoot behavior in combat. Power squares cycle every 8 turns between light, neutral, and dark.
