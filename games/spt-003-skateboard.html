SPT — Sports Prototype #003 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Ride a halfpipe, build speed by pumping, launch off the lip, and perform tricks in the air. Chain successful tricks for combo multipliers. Get the highest score in 60 seconds.

  • ← → — Pump / lean (press at bottom of curve to gain speed)
  • Space — Launch off the lip when near the top edge
  • Z — Kickflip (fast trick, 100 pts)
  • X — Grab (medium trick, 200 pts)
  • C — 360 Spin (slow trick, 300 pts)
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Pump timing: Press left/right at the bottom of the halfpipe to gain speed, like a real skater.
  • Height matters: More speed = higher air = more time for tricks = height bonus.
  • Commit to tricks: Tricks must finish before you land, or you crash and lose your combo.
  • Combos: Each consecutive trick increases the multiplier (x2, x3, x4...). A crash resets it.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: 720° (1986, Atari Games) → Skate or Die (1987, EA) → Tony Hawk's Pro Skater (1999)

720° (1986) was the first skateboarding arcade game, featuring an isometric view and a park full of events including halfpipe. Skate or Die (1987) by Electronic Arts brought skateboarding to home computers with multiple events including a halfpipe competition. Both games struggled with the fundamental challenge of translating the fluid, physics-driven nature of skateboarding into digital form.

The halfpipe event is the purest expression of skateboard game design: a constrained physics system (the U-shaped curve) where player skill manifests as understanding of momentum, timing, and risk management. Tony Hawk's Pro Skater (1999) would later revolutionize the genre by making trick chains the central scoring mechanic.

This resonance-exploitation pattern appears in control theory as energy pumping in nonlinear oscillators. The skateboard halfpipe is a physical pendulum with parametric excitation — the same mathematics that describes children pumping on swings.

The Convergence: Modern robotics uses the same energy-pumping principles for locomotion. Boston Dynamics' robots exploit resonant oscillations for efficient movement. The skater pumping a halfpipe and a robot optimizing its gait share the same underlying mathematics.

If AI Had Designed This: Optimal trick selection based on remaining air time, using trajectory prediction to determine which tricks can be completed before landing — essentially a real-time scheduling problem with kinematic constraints.
