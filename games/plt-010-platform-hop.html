PLT — Platformer Prototype #010 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Bounce endlessly upward on platforms. Your jumper auto-bounces when landing on a platform. Reach as high as you can without falling off the bottom of the screen.

  • ← → — Move left/right
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Platform Types

  • Green: Normal — always solid
  • Blue: Moving — slides left and right
  • Brown: Breakable — crumbles after one landing
  • Red: Spring — super bounce!

Tips

  • Screen wraps: Walk off the left edge to appear on the right, and vice versa.
  • Jump on enemies: Land on top of enemies to defeat them. Touching them from the side means death.
  • Plan ahead: Look for the next platform above you and aim for it.
  • Springs are gold: Red spring platforms launch you extra high — use them to skip difficult sections.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Doodle Jump (2009, Lima Sky) ← Icy Tower (2001, FreeLunchDesign) ← Ice Climber (1985, Nintendo)

Doodle Jump (2009) became one of the defining games of the early smartphone era. Created by Croatian-American brothers Igor and Marko Pusenjak at Lima Sky, its simple tilt-to-move mechanic and infinite vertical scrolling made it perfectly suited to mobile touchscreens.

The game distilled vertical platforming to its purest essence: auto-jumping, procedural platforms, and a single axis of player control. This simplicity made it endlessly accessible while the procedural generation ensured no two games were alike. It sold over 15 million copies and inspired countless imitators.

Its lineage traces through Icy Tower (2001), a Flash-era endless climber, back to Ice Climber (1985) on the NES, which first explored vertical platforming as a core mechanic. The genre evolved from designed levels to procedural infinity.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 5 — Deep Learning (2006–2020)

Contemporary AI: Deep learning revolution, generative adversarial networks, procedural content generation research.

Insight: Doodle Jump’s infinite procedural generation solved the content problem by making each playthrough unique — a constraint-based generative approach that predated modern procedural generation research in deep learning. The game’s difficulty curve emerges from platform distribution rules rather than hand-crafted levels, mirroring how generative models learn to produce varied outputs from learned distributions.

Classification

  • Secondary: Procedural generation, skill-based
  • Input: 2-axis horizontal movement
  • Perspective: Side-on, vertical scroll

Prototype Notes

This prototype implements the core vertical platformer loop with four platform types, procedural generation, screen wrapping, enemies, and score tracking. The difficulty gradually increases with altitude through platform spacing and enemy frequency.
