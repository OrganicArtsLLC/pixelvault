PUZ — Puzzle Prototype #012 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Deduce which cells to fill using the number clues along each row and column. The numbers indicate consecutive groups of filled cells in that line, separated by at least one empty cell.

  • Left click — Fill a cell (dark blue)
  • Right click — Mark a cell with X (definitely empty)
  • Click again — Clear a filled or marked cell
  • R — Restart current puzzle
  • N — Next puzzle
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Reading Clues

  • A clue of 3 1 means: a run of 3 filled cells, at least one gap, then 1 filled cell
  • A clue of 10 means the entire row/column is filled
  • A clue of 0 means the entire row/column is empty
  • Completed lines have their clues dimmed

Strategy Tips

  • Start with the largest clues — they have the fewest possible positions
  • Mark cells you know are empty with X to avoid mistakes
  • Look for overlapping possibilities to find guaranteed fills
  • Cross-reference row and column clues to narrow down cells

History & Lineage

Nonograms were invented independently in 1987 by Non Ishida in Japan and Gwynneth Flower in the UK. Ishida won a competition in a Tokyo newspaper with a picture puzzle inspired by skyscraper-lit windows. The name "nonogram" combines Non's name with "gram" (something drawn).

Nintendo's Picross series, developed by Jupiter Corporation, popularized the format starting with Mario's Picross (1995, Game Boy), which introduced the concept to Western audiences. The series has continued for decades across Nintendo platforms. The genre found new life on mobile platforms and in games like Paint It Back (2015) and Pictopix (2017).

The puzzle format has been adapted into color variants (multiple colors with separate clue sets), triddlers (triangular grids), and massive puzzles exceeding 100×100 cells.

AI Archaeology

Era 4 — Expert Boom: Nonogram solving demonstrates constraint propagation, a core AI technique. Each line (row or column) is treated as an independent constraint. A "line solver" enumerates valid arrangements for each line and identifies cells that must be filled or empty in all valid arrangements.

While the general nonogram problem is NP-complete (proven by Ueda and Nagao, 1996), well-designed puzzles are solvable by iterative line solving alone — no backtracking required. This makes them an excellent teaching tool for constraint satisfaction concepts.

The relationship between puzzle design and solvability is itself an interesting AI problem: generating puzzles that are uniquely solvable, aesthetically pleasing, and achievable without trial-and-error requires careful constraint engineering.
