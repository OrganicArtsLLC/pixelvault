TRP — Territorial Trap Prototype #003 ★★★★

How to Play

Divide the playing field by building walls to trap bouncing balls. Claim 75% of the area to clear each level.

  • Click — Start building a wall at cursor position
  • Tab — Toggle wall direction (Horizontal / Vertical)
  • R — Restart game
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Wall building: Walls extend in both directions from your click point. They stop when they reach an existing wall or the field border.
  • Danger: If a ball hits a wall while it's still building, the wall is destroyed and you lose a life.
  • Claiming: Once a wall completes, any enclosed region with no balls is claimed (filled in).
  • Strategy: Isolate balls into smaller areas. Build walls when balls are moving away from your click point.
  • Levels: Each level adds one more ball. Level 1 has 2 balls, level 2 has 3, etc.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Qix (1981, Randy & Sandy Pfeiffer) → Volfied (1989, Taito) → JezzBall (1992, Dima Pavlovsky) → Barrack (1998)

Qix (1981) by Randy and Sandy Pfeiffer established the territory-capture genre with freeform line-drawing. Volfied (1989, Taito) served as an intermediate link, bridging Qix and JezzBall by adding enemies inside the claiming area.

JezzBall (1992) was created by Dima Pavlovsky and included in Microsoft Entertainment Pack 3. It became one of the most-played Windows games of the 1990s, alongside Minesweeper and Solitaire. Where Qix demanded freeform line-drawing courage, JezzBall simplified the mechanic to axis-aligned walls. This made the spatial reasoning more accessible: you’re always building straight horizontal or vertical barriers. The tension shifts from “how do I draw my path” to “when and where do I place this wall.”

The game’s genius is in its constraint reduction. By limiting walls to two orientations, it focuses the player entirely on timing and spatial partitioning. The balls are obstacles, not enemies — you’re not fighting them, you’re building around them. Barrack (1998) extended the concept with powerups and varied ball behaviors.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (1978–1993)

Contemporary AI: By 1992, computational geometry was mature — BSP trees (used in Doom, 1993), quadtrees, and spatial indexing were well-understood. JezzBall's core mechanic IS spatial partitioning: each wall is a BSP split, and evaluating which regions contain balls is a point-in-polygon test.

Insight: The player performs real-time geometric reasoning: "If I place a wall here, which regions will be created? Will they contain balls?" This is the same reasoning that spatial databases use for range queries. The optimal strategy — maximizing claimed area while minimizing risk — maps to computational geometry optimization problems.

Modern relevance: JezzBall strategies could be solved with reinforcement learning on continuous state spaces, but the combinatorial explosion of wall placements across time makes it surprisingly hard for AI. The human intuition for "trap the ball in the corner" is spatial reasoning that deep learning struggles to replicate efficiently.

Classification

  • Series: TRP (Territorial Trap) — games about claiming and defending territory
  • Mechanic: Territory division through wall-building
  • Input: Mouse click + keyboard toggle
  • State space: Continuous (ball positions) + discrete (grid cells)
  • Challenge type: Spatial reasoning + timing

Prototype Notes

  • Field uses a grid internally for wall/claimed tracking, balls use continuous positions
  • Wall growth is animated at a fixed speed for visual feedback
  • Region detection uses flood fill after wall completion
  • Ball-wall collision checks both completed walls and in-progress walls
  • 75% threshold matches the original JezzBall requirement
