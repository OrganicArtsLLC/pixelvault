PUZ-030 · BALL MAZE

PUZ — Puzzle Prototype #030 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Rotate the entire maze to guide the marble to the exit hole. Gravity always pulls the marble straight down on screen — by rotating the maze, you change which surfaces the marble rolls along.

  • ← / → — Rotate maze counter-clockwise / clockwise
  • R — Restart current level
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Scoring

  • Each level has a countdown timer — reach the exit before time runs out
  • Green bumpers — add bonus time (+3s)
  • Red bumpers — subtract time (-2s)
  • Remaining time carries over as bonus points
  • 6 mazes of increasing complexity

History

Cameltry was released by Taito in 1989 as an arcade cabinet with a unique paddle controller for smooth maze rotation. The game was a critical darling for its innovative "rotate the world" mechanic. It was ported to SNES in 1992 (titled "On the Ball" in North America). The concept influenced later titles like Super Monkey Ball and Kororinpa.

The name "Cameltry" is a portmanteau — the exact origin is debated, but it captures the game's spirit of navigating through tight passages. The arcade version used analog paddle controls that made the rotation feel wonderfully precise.

AI Archaeology

Classification

Era 3 — Knowledge Explosion. Cameltry requires real-time physics simulation with coordinate frame transformation. The marble's position must be tracked in world space while gravity operates in screen space, requiring continuous rotation matrix application. This was computationally straightforward but conceptually sophisticated for 1989.

Mechanic DNA

The "rotate the world" mechanic creates a perceptual puzzle: players must think in two reference frames simultaneously. The marble obeys screen-space gravity while the maze rotates around it. This dual-frame thinking is what makes Cameltry feel unlike any other maze game.

Prototype Notes

This sketch captures the core rotation mechanic with 6 hand-designed mazes. Physics use simplified collision against axis-aligned segments rotated into screen space. The visual of the maze spinning while the marble drops straight down recreates the mesmerizing quality of the original.
