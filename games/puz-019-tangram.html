History

The tangram originated in China around 1800, though some historians trace it to the Song Dynasty. It consists of seven flat polygons called "tans" cut from a single square: 2 large right triangles, 1 medium right triangle, 2 small right triangles, 1 square, and 1 parallelogram. The puzzle spread to Europe in the early 19th century and became a worldwide craze. Napoleon reportedly played tangrams during his exile. Lewis Carroll and Edgar Allan Poe were also enthusiasts.

The 7 pieces can form over 6,500 distinct convex and non-convex shapes. The tangram is universal — it requires no language or literacy, making it one of the most culturally portable puzzles ever created.

AI Archaeology

Era 5 — Statistical Learning

Tangram solving is a geometric constraint satisfaction problem: 7 polygons must tile a target region with zero gaps and zero overlap. This is equivalent to polygon packing — an NP-hard problem. Modern approaches use computer vision for shape recognition, geometric constraint solvers for piece placement, and reinforcement learning for sequential assembly strategies.

Applications include robotic bin packing, sheet metal nesting optimization, and automated jigsaw puzzle assembly — all descendants of the humble tangram's spatial logic.
