History

Kuru Kuru Kururin was a launch title for the Game Boy Advance in Japan (2001), developed by Eighting (now M2) and published by Nintendo. The game was released in Europe but never in North America. Players guide the Helirin — a spinning stick-shaped vehicle — through maze-like courses. The brilliance lies in its simplicity: you only control position while the stick rotates automatically, creating an elegant timing puzzle from basic ingredients. The sequel, Kururin Paradise, added stick-stopping mechanics, but the original's purity of design remains its hallmark. The franchise is a cult favorite among import gamers and speedrunners.

AI Archaeology

AI Era 5 — Reasoning

Kururin presents a fascinating challenge for AI: the continuously rotating elongated hitbox transforms simple 2D pathfinding into a time-dependent spatial reasoning problem. An AI agent must predict future stick orientations along potential paths and plan trajectories that satisfy geometric constraints at every point in time. This periodic constraint satisfaction — where the "shape" of the agent changes cyclically — connects to robotics problems involving articulated bodies moving through confined spaces, and to temporal logic planning in AI research.

Prototype Notes

Six hand-crafted mazes progress from wide corridors to serpentine passages. The Helirin is drawn as a rainbow-striped stick with glowing endpoints. Rotation speed is constant across all levels. Camera follows the stick with the maze scrolling around it. Collision uses line-segment vs rectangle intersection for accuracy. The core challenge emerges purely from the interaction between movement and rotation.
