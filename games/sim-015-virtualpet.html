SIM-015 · VIRTUAL PET

SIM — Simulation Prototype #015 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Care for a tiny person living in a three-story house. They act autonomously — your job is to keep them happy.

  • Click character — pet them (+happiness)
  • Click fridge — drop food (+hunger satisfaction)
  • Click radio — play music (+happiness)
  • Click bathtub — run a bath (+hygiene)
  • Click bookshelf — suggest reading (+happiness)
  • ? — toggle this info panel

There is no win or lose condition. Just watch, interact, and care.

History

Little Computer People (1985) by Rich Gold and David Crane at Activision was a radical departure from conventional gaming. Instead of controlling a character, players observed and interacted with a small person who lived inside the computer — complete with their own house, daily routine, and emotional states.

Each copy of the game generated a unique character with different personality traits, making every player's experience personal. The character would write letters to the player, play piano, exercise, and sometimes just sit and think.

This concept directly inspired Tamagotchi (1996), which distilled virtual pet care into a keychain device, and The Sims (2000), which expanded autonomous agent behavior into a full life simulation. The through-line from Little Computer People to modern virtual pets is one of gaming's clearest evolutionary paths.

AI Archaeology

Autonomous Agent Behavior

The character uses a need-driven decision system — the earliest form of utility AI in games. Each need (hunger, energy, happiness, hygiene) decays over time, and the character prioritizes actions based on which need is most critical. This is the same architecture Will Wright later formalized in The Sims.

Emergent Personality

By varying decision thresholds and action preferences, each character appears to have a unique personality — an early demonstration that simple rule variations can produce the illusion of complex individuality.

Prototype Notes

This sketch captures the core loop of virtual pet care: autonomous character with decaying needs, player interaction through environmental objects, and the gentle satisfaction of watching a tiny life unfold. No death state — the character gets sad but never perishes, keeping the tone warm and inviting.
