How to Play

Collect all coins to clear the level. Place temporary doors to block police pursuers. Grab treasure items for bonus points.

  • ↑ ↓ ← → — Move through the maze
  • Space — Place a door behind you to block pursuers
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Door placement: You can hold up to 3 doors. They recharge over time. Doors vanish after ~5 seconds.
  • Trap police: Place doors in corridors behind you to cut off pursuit. Time it right to trap them in dead ends.
  • Treasure: Bonus items appear periodically in the maze center. Grab them quickly for big points.
  • Clearing levels: Each level increases police speed and reduces door duration. Plan your routes.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Lock 'n' Chase (1981, Data East) ← Pac-Man (1980, Namco)

Lock 'n' Chase (1981) was Data East's response to Pac-Man, replacing the ghost-evading pellet-eater with a thief-vs-police scenario. The key innovation was the door-locking mechanic: the player could place temporary barriers in the maze to block pursuing police officers.

This seemingly small addition transformed the game from pure evasion into a strategic planning problem. Players had to think ahead — not just "where am I going?" but "where will I block?" The ability to reshape the environment in real-time was revolutionary for 1981.

The game featured a vault-robbery theme with coins and treasure, giving it a distinct identity from Pac-Man's abstract dot-eating. It saw ports to Atari 2600, Intellivision, and Apple II.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 2 — Expert Systems (1972–1990)

Contemporary AI: Rule-based pursuit algorithms. Police use simple chase logic with basic obstacle avoidance.

Hidden Intelligence: Lock 'n' Chase introduces what modern AI calls "dynamic environment manipulation." The player doesn't just navigate the state space — they modify it. This is conceptually related to option frameworks in hierarchical RL, where an agent can create or destroy affordances. The door-placement mechanic is a primitive form of environment design.

The Convergence: The interplay between pursuit AI and player-created obstacles presages adversarial environment design. Modern research in procedural content generation and adversarial training explores exactly this dynamic — agents that reshape their environment to achieve goals.

If AI Had Designed This: Adaptive police AI that learns the player's door-placement patterns and routes around anticipated blockades. Dynamic coin placement that rewards exploration of under-visited maze regions.
