SPT-028 · RHYTHM KEYS

SPT — Sports Prototype #028 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Notes fall down 6 lanes. Press the matching key when a note crosses the judgment line at the bottom. Hold long notes for their full duration.

  • S — Lane 1
  • D — Lane 2
  • F — Lane 3
  • J — Lane 4
  • K — Lane 5
  • L — Lane 6
  • Space — Start / Pause
  • R — Restart track
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Timing: Perfect (±35ms) 300 pts, Great (±70ms) 200 pts, Good (±110ms) 100 pts, Bad (±150ms) 50 pts. Misses drain the groove gauge. Keep the gauge above zero to clear the track!

History

DJMAX (디제이맥스, 2004) was created by Pentavision in South Korea, originally for the PlayStation Portable. It emerged from the Korean rhythm game scene alongside EZ2DJ (1999) and O2Jam (2002), but distinguished itself with a premium aesthetic — sleek UI design, licensed K-pop and electronic music, and music video backgrounds.

The series offered 4, 5, 6, and 8-button modes, with 6-button becoming the competitive standard. Unlike Japanese arcade rhythm games like Beatmania IIDX which used a turntable, DJMAX focused on pure key input, making it feel more like playing a keyboard instrument.

DJMAX Respect (2017) brought the series to PS4 and later PC, preserving the premium feel with 4K visuals and an enormous licensed soundtrack spanning K-pop, trance, drum and bass, and orchestral genres.

AI Archaeology

Era 6 — Transformer Era

DJMAX's 6-lane layout forces bimanual coordination — left hand on S/D/F, right hand on J/K/L — creating a split-attention problem analogous to multi-head attention in transformers. The player must simultaneously track falling notes across all lanes while maintaining held long notes, a working memory task that scales with lane count.

Long notes introduce a state-tracking requirement: the system must know which lanes are currently held and for how long, adding temporal persistence to the otherwise reactive input stream. This mirrors how transformer architectures maintain context across sequence positions.
