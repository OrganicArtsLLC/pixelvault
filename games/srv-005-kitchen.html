SRV — Service / Serve 'em Up Prototype #004 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Run a kitchen! Grab ingredients, cook and chop them at stations, assemble plates, and serve orders before they expire. Manage multiple orders under time pressure.

  • ← ↑ → ↓ or WASD — Move chef
  • Space — Pick up / put down / interact with station
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Read the orders: Tickets at the top show what each customer wants. The timer bar shows urgency.
  • Cook meat on the stove: Place raw meat on the stove and wait. But don't leave it too long — it burns!
  • Chop veggies on the cutting board: Place lettuce, tomato, or cheese on the cutting board to prepare them.
  • Assemble on plates: Place prepared items on an empty plate at the counter. Build the recipe in any order.
  • Serve at the window: Carry a completed plate to the serving window to fulfill an order.
  • Trash mistakes: Carry burnt food or wrong items to the trash can to dispose of them.
  • Speed tips: Faster service earns bonus tip points. Plan your route through the kitchen!

History & Lineage

Ancestry: BurgerTime (1982) → Cooking Mama (2006) → Overcooked (2016) → PlateUp! (2022)

BurgerTime (1982) by Data East is the genre’s ancestor, casting the player as a chef assembling burgers while dodging food enemies. Cooking Mama (2006) by Office Create on the DS focused on the tactile joy of individual cooking steps, bringing kitchen gameplay to a wider audience.

Overcooked (2016) by Phil Duncan and Ol De-Gaulle at Ghost Town Games, published by Team17, turned kitchen service into cooperative chaos, requiring players to coordinate ingredient prep, cooking, plating, and serving across increasingly absurd kitchen layouts. PlateUp! (2022) by It’s Happening added roguelike elements and kitchen customization. The throughline from BurgerTime to PlateUp! is the satisfaction of completing complex multi-step processes under time pressure.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 6 — Deep Learning (2012-present)

Contemporary AI: Deep reinforcement learning, transformer architectures, multi-agent coordination systems.

Hidden Intelligence: Kitchen service is a real-time scheduling problem with resource constraints. Multiple orders compete for limited cooking stations, ingredients, and player time. The player must solve a dynamic job-shop scheduling problem — the same NP-hard optimization problem that factory automation and logistics AI tackles. Each order is a job with ordered operations (chop, cook, plate), each requiring specific machines (stations) for specific durations. The player is the scheduler, and unlike algorithmic solvers, must also physically move between stations.

Modern Parallel: Amazon warehouse robots solve a similar problem — moving items between stations in optimal order. DeepMind's AlphaStar demonstrated that real-time resource management and multi-task scheduling can be learned by neural networks, achieving superhuman performance in StarCraft II's macro-management.

Prototype Notes

Single-file HTML5 Canvas implementation. Top-down kitchen layout with ingredient stations, cooking stations, plating counters, serving window, and trash. Four recipe types: Burger, Salad, Sandwich, and Soup. Burning mechanic on stove. 2.5-minute rounds with progressive difficulty (more orders, shorter timers).
