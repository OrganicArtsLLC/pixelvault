PUZ-020 · CONCENTRATION

PUZ — Puzzle Prototype #020 ★★★★

How to Play

Find all 8 matching pairs of cards by flipping them two at a time. Remember where each symbol is located!

  • Click a face-down card to flip it
  • Flip two cards per turn — if they match, they stay face-up
  • If they don’t match, they flip back after a moment
  • Find all 8 pairs to win
  • R — Start a new game
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Focus on a few positions at a time — don’t try to memorize every card at once
  • Pay attention to cards your opponent (your past self) has already revealed
  • Work systematically through rows or columns
  • Fewer moves = better score

History & Lineage

Concentration (also called Memory, Pelmanism, or Pairs) likely originated as a parlor game in the 1700s. The mechanic is ancient: lay cards face-down, turn them over two at a time, keep matching pairs. Its simplicity makes it playable by very young children, yet the combinatorial explosion of positions makes large grids genuinely challenging for adults.

Ravensburger published the definitive commercial version as Memory in 1959, designed by William Hurter. It became one of the best-selling games of all time, with over 75 million copies sold across dozens of themed editions. The American TV game show Concentration (1958–1991) added a rebus puzzle layer to the matching mechanic.

In video games, memory matching appears as a mini-game in countless titles — from Super Mario Bros. 3’s card-matching bonus to countless mobile games. Its pure test of short-term memory makes it a staple of cognitive training applications.

AI Archaeology

Era 2 — Expert Systems (associative memory, pattern matching).

Concentration directly tests spatial working memory — the ability to bind objects to locations and recall them after interference. Cognitive science uses it extensively to study memory decay, proactive interference, and age-related cognitive decline.

An AI player with perfect memory solves Concentration trivially: record every revealed card, match on second sight. The challenge for humans is that our working memory has limited capacity (Miller’s 7±2 items) and suffers from interference when new information displaces old. Human strategies — spatial chunking, narrative encoding, deliberate rehearsal — mirror the cache management strategies that computer architectures use.

The game’s difficulty scales with grid size in the same way that memory span experiments do, making it a naturalistic cognitive assessment tool.
