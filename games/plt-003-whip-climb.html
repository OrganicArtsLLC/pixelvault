PLT-003 · WHIP CLIMB

PLT — Platformer Prototype #003 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Guide a whip-wielding hero through a gothic castle. Destroy candles for hearts and subweapons. Defeat the boss at the end of the level.

  • ← → — Move left/right
  • ↑ ↓ — Go up/down stairs, crouch
  • Space — Jump (stiff — limited air control)
  • Z — Whip attack
  • X — Use subweapon (costs hearts)
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Stiff jumps: Once airborne, your horizontal direction is locked. Commit before you leap.
  • Whip range: The whip reaches further than your body — use spacing to your advantage.
  • Subweapons: Knife (straight), Axe (arcing), Holy Water (ground fire). Each costs 1 heart.
  • Candles: Whip every candle — they drop hearts, subweapons, and points.
  • Stairs: Press Up/Down while standing on stair tiles to climb.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Castlevania (1986, Konami) → Super Castlevania IV (1991) → Symphony of the Night (1997) → Metroidvania genre

Castlevania (1986) by Konami inverted the power fantasy. Where Mario felt nimble and responsive, Simon Belmont felt heavy and deliberate. This was not a limitation — it was the design. Every jump was a commitment. Every whip crack had startup frames. The horror came not from surprise but from being unable to change your mind once you committed to an action.

The whip created a spacing game: you could hit things further away than you could be hit, but only if you timed it right. This risk/reward calculation — stand at range and whip, or rush in and risk contact — is the mechanical heart of the game.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (1978–1983, influence through 1986)

Contemporary AI: Expert systems were dominant. Scripted behavior was the state of the art for game AI. Backpropagation had just been popularized (1986).

Hidden Intelligence: Castlevania's stiff controls encode a planning constraint: each input is a commitment, not a correction. The player must observe enemy patterns, predict trajectories, and commit to actions before executing them. This is the explore-exploit tradeoff made visceral — you must gather information (observe) before acting (attack/jump). The level design functions as a curriculum: early rooms teach mechanics in low-stakes settings before combining them in deadly arrangements.

The Convergence: Modern RL agents struggle with games requiring commitment-based action — most policies assume instant course correction. Castlevania-style games require planning horizons longer than single frames, closer to model-based RL or Monte Carlo Tree Search than reactive policy networks.

If AI Had Designed This: Adaptive difficulty through enemy pattern modulation — varying sine wave frequencies, spawn timing, and projectile speeds to maintain a target challenge level.

Classification

Series: PLT — Platformer

Castlevania is a platformer that replaces nimble movement with deliberate commitment. The whip extends the action space beyond the player's hitbox, creating a spacing mechanic unique to this lineage.

Core Mechanic

Whip-based platformer with stiff jumps and subweapons. Medium-range whip attack. Candle-breaking for pickups. Hearts as subweapon ammo. Scripted enemy patterns (sine waves, arcs). Boss encounter. Stair traversal.

What This Prototype Tests

  • Stiff jumps — does commitment-based movement create tension?
  • Whip range — does spacing feel distinct from contact combat?
  • Subweapon variety — do knife/axe/holy water feel meaningfully different?
  • Gothic atmosphere — does the palette sell the mood?
