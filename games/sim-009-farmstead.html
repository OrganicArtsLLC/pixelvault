SIM — Simulation Prototype #009 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Run a small farm: till soil, plant seeds, water crops, harvest produce, and sell at market. Manage your energy and gold to expand.

  • Arrow keys / WASD — Move your farmer
  • 1 — Hoe: till grass into farmable soil (costs 5 energy)
  • 2 — Seeds: plant on tilled soil (costs 3 energy + seed price)
  • 3 — Watering Can: water planted crops (costs 2 energy)
  • 4 — Harvest: collect ready crops (costs 2 energy)
  • Space — Use selected tool on current tile
  • E — Sell all harvested crops at market
  • Q/Tab — Cycle seed type when seed tool is active
  • N — Sleep / advance to next day (restores energy)
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Crops

  • Turnip — Seed: 5g, Sell: 15g, Grows in 3 days. Fast starter crop.
  • Potato — Seed: 10g, Sell: 30g, Grows in 5 days. Reliable income.
  • Tomato — Seed: 20g, Sell: 55g, Grows in 7 days. Good profit margin.
  • Corn — Seed: 35g, Sell: 100g, Grows in 10 days. Premium investment.

Tips

  • Watered crops grow twice as fast — water daily for best results.
  • You start with 100 energy per day. Sleep to restore it.
  • Start with turnips for quick cash, then invest in premium crops.
  • Walk to the market stall (top-right) and press E to sell.

History & Lineage

Harvest Moon (1996) was created by Yasuhiro Wada at Pack-in-Video (later Victor Interactive Software) for SNES, inventing the farming simulation genre and proving that the mundane rhythms of agricultural life could be deeply compelling as gameplay. Wada combined farming with social simulation and time management in a way no game had attempted before.

FarmVille (2009) by Mark Pincus at Zynga distilled the farming loop into a social/mobile format, becoming one of the most-played games ever made on Facebook, though criticized for exploitative monetization patterns.

Stardew Valley (2016) by Eric "ConcernedApe" Barone, a solo developer who built the entire game over four years, became a cultural phenomenon selling over 30 million copies. It proved that one person could create a game rivaling major studio output, and revitalized the farming sim genre.

AI Archaeology

The farming cycle is a multi-objective optimization problem. The player must balance competing resources (time, energy, gold, land) while navigating seasonal constraints. This mirrors the explore-exploit tradeoff in reinforcement learning: should you plant safe turnips or invest in risky corn?

The invest→wait→harvest→reinvest loop creates a compounding returns structure identical to financial optimization. The "just one more day" pull of farming games comes from this exponential growth curve — the same mathematical structure that makes compound interest so powerful.

AI Era 6 — Deep Learning. Modern farming game AI could use neural networks to predict optimal crop rotations, dynamic pricing models, and personalized difficulty curves that keep each player in their flow state.

Prototype Notes

This prototype captures the core plant-tend-harvest-sell loop in a minimal top-down format. The 12×8 grid provides enough space for meaningful farm layout decisions. Energy and day cycles create the pacing that makes farming sims compelling — you can never do everything in one day, forcing prioritization.

Intentionally omitted: seasons with crop restrictions, animal husbandry, NPC relationships, crafting, fishing, mining. Each of these could be a prototype extension.
