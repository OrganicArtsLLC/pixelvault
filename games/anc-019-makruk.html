How to Play

Play Makruk (Thai Chess) against an AI opponent. You are White (bottom). Click a piece to see legal moves highlighted, then click a destination to move.

  • Bia (Pawn): Moves forward one square, captures diagonally forward. Starts on 3rd rank. Promotes to Met when reaching the 6th rank (opponent's 3rd rank).
  • Met (Queen): Moves one square diagonally only. The weakest piece after the pawn.
  • Khon (Bishop): Moves one square diagonally OR one square straight forward. Cannot move backward or sideways (except diagonally).
  • Ma (Knight): Moves in an L-shape, same as Western chess. Can jump over pieces.
  • Rua (Rook): Moves any number of squares horizontally or vertically, same as Western chess.
  • Khun (King): Moves one square in any direction, same as Western chess.
  • No castling, no en passant, no two-square pawn advance.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Chaturanga (~600 CE, India) → Makruk (~1000 CE, Thailand)

Makruk is the closest living relative of the original Indian game Chaturanga. While Western chess evolved through Persian Shatranj and gained powerful long-range queens and bishops around 1475, Makruk preserved the ancient weak pieces. The Met (queen) moves only one square diagonally, and the Khon (bishop) moves one square diagonally or one forward — almost exactly matching Chaturanga's counselor and elephant.

The game is still widely played in Thailand and Cambodia (where it is called Ouk Chatrang). It is recognized by FIDE and has an active competitive scene. Makruk's slower pace and emphasis on endgame technique make it a fascinating contrast to the tactical fireworks of modern Western chess.

Key Differences from Western Chess: Pawns start on the 3rd rank (already advanced). Promotion yields only a Met (weak queen). No long-range bishops. No castling. The result is a game where piece coordination and king activity matter far more than in Western chess.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 2 — Traditional (~1000 CE)

Makruk represents a fork in chess evolution where the pieces stayed weak. This makes it an interesting case study for game AI: without powerful long-range pieces, the branching factor is lower but positional understanding becomes more critical. Material advantages are harder to convert because the pieces are less powerful.

Modern chess engines can play Makruk at superhuman level, but the game's character — slow maneuvering, king centralization, pawn structure — rewards a different style of evaluation than Western chess. The endgame counting rules (similar to the 50-move rule but with specific piece-dependent limits) add unique complexity.

Classification

Series: ANC — Ancient Games

Games with roots stretching back centuries or millennia. Makruk is a living fossil — a direct window into how chess was played before the European revolution of the 15th century.

Core Mechanic

Perfect information, turn-based, piece-differentiated movement, capture by replacement. Weaker pieces create a slower, more positional game than Western chess.
