How to Play

Surakarta is a two-player Indonesian strategy game on a 6×6 grid with curved orbital loops at each corner. You play as Red (bottom). Click a piece to select it, then click a destination.

  • Normal Move: Move one step in any direction (8 directions) to an empty square. Normal moves CANNOT capture.
  • Orbital Capture: A piece travels along one or more curved loop arcs and lands on an enemy piece to capture it. The piece must pass through at least one loop arc.
  • Win: Capture all enemy pieces, or leave the opponent with no legal moves.
  • R — Restart game

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Surakarta (traditional, Java, Indonesia)

Named after the Javanese city of Surakarta (also called Solo), this game is believed to have been played in the royal courts of the Surakarta Sultanate. The game's most distinctive feature — its curved orbital capture paths — has no parallel in any other known board game tradition.

The board features 8 curved arcs (2 per corner, an inner and outer loop) that create circular paths around the board edges. Pieces can only capture by traveling along these curved paths, making spatial reasoning fundamentally different from chess or checkers. A piece might travel halfway around the board along the loops to reach and capture an enemy.

The game spread throughout Southeast Asia and gained international attention in the 20th century when game theorists recognized its unique capture mechanic as computationally interesting.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 2 — Early Expert Systems

Surakarta presents a fascinating challenge for AI because the orbital capture mechanic requires path-finding along curved loops rather than simple grid adjacency. Traditional minimax works, but the branching factor for captures is unusual — a single piece might have capture paths that travel through multiple loop arcs to reach distant targets.

The game has been studied computationally since the 2000s, with researchers noting that the loop-based capture makes standard board game heuristics less effective. Position evaluation must account for which pieces are "exposed" to loop paths — a concept with no analog in other abstract strategy games.
