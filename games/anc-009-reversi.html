How to Play

Play Reversi (Othello) against an AI opponent. You are Black and move first. Place discs to outflank your opponent's pieces — flanked discs flip to your color.

  • Place: Click any highlighted square (semi-transparent dots show legal moves).
  • Flanking: Your disc must bracket opponent discs in a straight line (horizontal, vertical, or diagonal) with one of your existing pieces on the other end.
  • Passing: If you have no legal moves, your turn automatically passes.
  • Winning: The game ends when the board is full or neither player can move. Most discs wins.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Reversi (1883, Lewis Waterman & John Mollett) → Othello (1971, Goro Hasegawa) → Computer Othello tournaments (1980s–present)

Reversi was invented in England in 1883, with both Lewis Waterman and John Mollett claiming credit — a dispute never fully resolved. The game languished in moderate popularity until Goro Hasegawa, a Japanese salesman, streamlined the rules in 1971, fixed the starting position (the four center pieces), and trademarked it as "Othello" with the tagline "A minute to learn, a lifetime to master."

The name "Othello" references Shakespeare's play about the Moor of Venice — a drama of black and white, reversal and betrayal. The green board represents the fields outside Venice.

Competitive Play: The World Othello Championship has been held annually since 1977. Unlike chess, where computers gradually overtook humans, Othello's smaller game tree (roughly 10^28 legal positions) meant computers dominated earlier and more decisively.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 4 — Expert Boom (positional evaluation and endgame solving)

Logistello (1997): Michael Buro's Logistello program defeated the reigning world champion Takeshi Murakami 6-0 in a landmark match. Unlike Deep Blue's brute-force approach to chess that same year, Logistello relied on learned evaluation functions — machine-tuned positional weights derived from hundreds of thousands of expert games.

The Positional Hierarchy: Othello's AI insight is elegant in its clarity:

  • Corners (A1, A8, H1, H8): Maximally valuable — once taken, a corner can never be flipped. Corners anchor stable disc chains.
  • Edges: Strong positions that are hard (but not impossible) to flip.
  • X-squares (B2, B7, G2, G7): Dangerous — placing here before the adjacent corner is taken gives your opponent corner access.
  • C-squares (A2, B1, etc.): Also risky early, as they can expose corners.

This positional hierarchy maps directly to feature-weighted evaluation functions — the same technique used in all modern game AI. The board is divided into features (corner control, edge stability, mobility), each assigned a learned weight. The weighted sum becomes the position's score.

Classification

Mechanic: Flanking capture — place discs to outflank opponent's pieces

Perfect Information: Yes — all game state is visible to both players.

Solved: Weakly solved for some board sizes. 8×8 is not fully solved but computers play optimally in practice.

Prototype Notes

This prototype implements an AI opponent using positional weight tables inspired by Logistello's approach. The AI evaluates corner control, edge stability, mobility (number of legal moves), and disc count. It uses minimax search with alpha-beta pruning to look several moves ahead.
