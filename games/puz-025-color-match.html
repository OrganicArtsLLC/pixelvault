How to Play

Move colored balls on a 9x9 grid to form lines of 5 or more same-colored balls. Lines can be horizontal, vertical, or diagonal.

  • Click a ball to select it (it bounces to show selection)
  • Click an empty cell to move the selected ball there
  • The ball must have a clear path — it cannot jump over other balls
  • If you form a line of 5+, those balls are cleared and you score points
  • If no line is formed, 3 new balls appear on the grid
  • Small dots show where the next 3 balls will appear
  • Game ends when the grid is completely full

Scoring

  • 5 in a line = 10 points
  • 6 in a line = 20 points
  • 7 in a line = 40 points
  • 8 in a line = 80 points
  • 9 in a line = 160 points
  • Multiple lines cleared in one move stack!

History

Color Lines was created in 1992 by Olga Demina, Gennady Denisov, and Igor Ivkin at Gamos studio in Moscow. It became one of the most popular casual games in the former Soviet Union and Eastern Europe, pre-installed on countless Windows machines. The game's genius is its simplicity — the rules take seconds to learn, but the pathfinding constraint and random spawns create deep strategic tension. It spawned dozens of clones including Lines 98, Winlines, and many mobile versions.

AI Archaeology

Pathfinding as Puzzle Constraint

The BFS (breadth-first search) reachability check is the game's secret weapon. Without it, Color Lines would be a trivial matching game. The pathfinding requirement means every ball placement is a spatial logistics problem — you must consider not just where you want the ball, but whether it can physically get there. This transforms color matching into route planning.

Entropy and the Tightening Noose

Each failed move adds 3 balls, steadily filling the grid. The preview mechanic gives players partial information about incoming randomness, creating a planning horizon. Expert play involves sacrificing immediate matches to keep paths open — a form of resource management where the resource is empty space itself.

Prototype Notes

This prototype implements BFS pathfinding for move validation, 7-color ball system with animated selection bounce, next-ball preview, and diagonal line detection. The grid rendering uses a warm wood-tone palette reminiscent of the original Windows versions.
