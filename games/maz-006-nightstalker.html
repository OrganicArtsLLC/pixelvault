How to Play

Navigate the dark maze, retrieve the gun from the center, and survive escalating waves of enemies. When you run out of ammo, return to the center for a new gun.

  • Space — Shoot (in facing direction; requires gun)

Tips

  • Gun at center: The gun spawns at the maze center. Walk over it to pick it up. You get 6 shots.
  • Ammo is scarce: When your 6 shots are spent, you are defenseless. Run back to center for a new gun.
  • Spider webs: Spiders leave webs that slow you down. Avoid them when fleeing unarmed.
  • Enemy escalation: Early waves are spiders. Then bats appear. Then robots that shoot back.
  • Darkness: Your vision is limited. Enemies lurk beyond the light.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Night Stalker (1982, Mattel/Intellivision) ← Berzerk (1980, Stern)

Night Stalker (1982) was a top-down maze shooter for the Intellivision that introduced resource scarcity to the arena shooter formula. Unlike Berzerk's unlimited firepower, Night Stalker gave the player a gun with only 6 bullets. When empty, the player had to retrieve a new gun from the maze center — completely defenseless during the trip.

The game featured three distinct enemy types — spiders, bats, and robots — each with different behaviors. This multi-agent ecosystem created emergent difficulty: a spider might block your path to the gun while a robot closes in from behind. The interplay between enemy types was more sophisticated than any single enemy's behavior.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 2 — Expert Systems (state-machine enemy behavior)

Contemporary AI: Rule-based expert systems. Finite state machines for character behavior were cutting-edge.

Hidden Intelligence: Each enemy type in Night Stalker operates on a simple state machine — patrol, chase, retreat. But the collective behavior of multiple agents with different rules creates emergent complexity. A spider's random wandering can accidentally herd the player toward a robot. A bat's erratic flight can cut off escape routes. This is an early example of multi-agent systems where simple individual rules produce complex collective behavior — a concept that wouldn't be formally studied until the late 1980s.

The Convergence: Night Stalker's resource scarcity (limited ammo, central gun spawn) anticipates modern game design patterns around resource management and risk/reward loops. The defenseless-to-armed-to-defenseless cycle creates a natural rhythm that modern designers call a "tension curve."

If AI Had Designed This: Dynamic difficulty adjustment — calibrating enemy spawn rates and types to maintain optimal tension. Procedural maze generation that guarantees interesting escape routes while preventing trivial camping spots.
