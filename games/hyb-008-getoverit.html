HYB-008 · GETTING OVER IT

HYB — Hybrid Prototype #008 ★★★★☆

How to Play

You are a person sitting in a cauldron, armed with a sledgehammer. Your goal: climb the mountain.

  • Mouse — Aim the hammer (it follows your cursor relative to the character)
  • Click & hold — The hammer grips surfaces; push against terrain to propel yourself
  • R — Restart from the bottom
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Slow, deliberate movements work better than frantic swinging
  • Use the hammer to hook over ledges and pull yourself up
  • There are no checkpoints — falling is always a real threat
  • The mountain has distinct sections: rocks, overhangs, and ice
  • Height is displayed in the corner — your personal best is tracked

History

Getting Over It with Bennett Foddy (2017) is a rage game about climbing a mountain using a sledgehammer while sitting in a cauldron. Inspired by Jazzuo's 2002 B-game Sexy Hiking, Foddy designed it as a meditation on failure, perseverance, and the relationship between player and designer. The game became a streaming phenomenon precisely because of its punishing lack of checkpoints.

The game belongs to a lineage of “masocore” or deliberately difficult games including I Wanna Be the Guy (2007), Super Meat Boy (2010), and QWOP (2008, also by Foddy). What sets Getting Over It apart is that the difficulty comes entirely from physics rather than enemies or traps.

AI Archaeology

Getting Over It represents AI Era 6 thinking about game design as emotional architecture. The “AI” of this game is the mountain itself — a carefully designed adversarial environment where physics creates emergent difficulty. Every surface angle, every overhang placement, is a decision by an invisible intelligence (the designer) to create specific emotional responses. This prefigures modern procedural difficulty systems and adaptive game AI.

Classification

  • Mechanics: Rage climbing, physics-based movement, lever mechanics, no-checkpoint progression
  • Lineage: Sexy Hiking → QWOP → Getting Over It → Jump King → Pogostuck

Prototype Notes

This is a sketch rated 4/5. The core hammer-physics mechanic captures the essence of Getting Over It: mouse-aimed lever action against terrain surfaces. The mountain is hand-designed with ~3000 pixels of vertical terrain across three zones (rocks, overhangs, ice). Physics are simplified but preserve the feel — hammer contact creates opposite force on the cauldron. No checkpoints. Height tracking shows progress and personal best.
