DEF-002 · BRICK BREAK

DEF — Deflection Prototype #002 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Bounce the ball off your paddle to destroy all bricks. Don't let the ball fall off the bottom.

  • ← → or A D — Move paddle
  • Space — Launch ball
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Angle control: Hit the ball with the paddle edge for steep angles, center for straight shots
  • Multi-hit bricks: Top rows take 3 hits (they dim and crack), bottom rows take 1
  • Top rows score more points

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Pong (1972) → Atari Breakout (1976) → Arkanoid (1986)

Breakout asked: what if there was no opponent, just a wall to destroy? Steve Wozniak built the original hardware prototype for Atari. The game introduced destructible environments — the world changes as you play. Arkanoid (1986) added power-ups, boss fights, and enemies, inventing the "brick-breaker" genre.

The core insight: as bricks disappear, the ball's path becomes less predictable. The game gets simultaneously easier (fewer targets) and harder (wilder trajectories). That tension is the design.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 2 — Expert Systems (1972–1977)

Contemporary AI: Same era as Pong. Rule-based systems, no learning. AI could match patterns but not explore state spaces.

Hidden Intelligence: Breakout removes the AI opponent entirely — physics IS the antagonist. The destructible brick field foreshadows procedural destruction by 30+ years. DeepMind's DQN (2013) later discovered the "tunnel behind the wall" strategy — a move humans knew intuitively but no one had formally documented until AI found it.

The Convergence: You don't need an intelligent opponent to create depth. The environment itself can be the challenge. AI rediscovering human intuition through brute-force search is its own convergence point.

If AI Had Designed This: Procedural brick layouts optimized for "interesting bounces" — 20 years before procedural generation existed as a concept.

Classification

Series: DEF — Deflection

Games built around redirecting a moving object. Breakout extends Pong by adding a destructible target field, creating a single-player loop from a multiplayer mechanic.

Core Mechanic

Deflection + destruction. Angle-based paddle control. Multi-HP bricks create shot planning depth. The thinning brick field increases ball chaos.

What This Prototype Tests

  • Brick HP systems (does multi-hit create satisfying progression?)
  • AABB-circle collision (does the ball behave predictably off bricks?)
  • The "tension peak" — moment when few bricks remain and the ball is hardest to control

Prototype Notes

This is a sketch — a mechanical skeleton. The goal is to feel the destructible-environment loop from the builder's perspective.
