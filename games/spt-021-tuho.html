How to Play

Throw thin arrows into a narrow-necked pot from a distance. Aim carefully — the opening is small!

  • Click+Drag — Set angle and power (drag away from pot)
  • Release — Throw the arrow
  • R — New game
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

You have 10 arrows per round. After your turn, an AI opponent throws 10 arrows. The player with more arrows in the pot wins. The pot neck is narrow — precision matters more than power.

History

Tuho (투호) is a traditional Korean arrow-throwing game dating back to at least the 5th century CE. It evolved from the Chinese game Touhu (投壺), which originated during the Warring States period (~5th century BCE) as a banquet entertainment for nobility. The game spread to Japan as Toko.

In Korea, Tuho became closely associated with Confucian court culture during the Joseon dynasty (1392–1897). It was considered a test of composure, grace, and self-control — not athletic prowess. Players stood at a fixed distance and threw thin bamboo arrows into a narrow-necked bronze or ceramic pot. The game appears in the Akhak Gwebeom (1493), the definitive Joseon text on court music and ritual.

Unlike Western dart games which reward accuracy on a target, Tuho demands precise arc control — the arrow must enter a vertical opening from above at the correct angle and speed. Too fast and it bounces out; too slow and it falls short.

AI Archaeology

Era 2 — Expert Systems

Tuho is a classic ballistic optimization problem. Given known physics (gravity, drag), the optimal throw can be calculated analytically — find the angle and velocity that place the arrow within the pot opening. An expert system can solve this perfectly, making AI opponents trivially strong. The human challenge is pure motor control: translating known physics into consistent muscle memory under the pressure of competition.

This mirrors the broader AI insight that many physical skill games are computationally simple but motorically demanding — the gap between knowing the solution and executing it is the entire game.
