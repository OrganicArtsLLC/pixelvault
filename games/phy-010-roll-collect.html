How to Play

Roll your katamari (sticky ball) around the world and pick up objects smaller than yourself. As you grow, bigger objects become collectible. Get as large as possible in 3 minutes!

History

Katamari Damacy (2004) was created by Keita Takahashi at Namco for the PlayStation 2. Born from Takahashi’s art school background, the game rejected the violence and complexity dominating the industry. Instead, it offered a single, joyful mechanic: roll a sticky ball that picks up everything it touches.

The game’s brilliance lies in its exponential growth curve. Starting with thumbtacks and dice, players eventually roll up people, buildings, and entire islands. The scale shift creates a unique sense of power progression that feels earned through accumulation rather than combat.

Originally a student project pitched at a Namco internal game jam, it was nearly cancelled multiple times due to its unconventional design. It became a cult classic and spawned numerous sequels. The soundtrack, mixing jazz, bossa nova, and J-pop, became iconic in its own right.

AI Archaeology

Spatial Reasoning & Growth Modeling

Katamari’s core loop is a continuous size-comparison problem. An AI agent must maintain a spatial model of every object’s size relative to the katamari’s current radius. This mirrors how computer vision systems learn hierarchical feature extraction — small features compose into larger structures.

Emergent Complexity from Simple Rules

The single rule “absorb anything smaller” creates emergent gameplay that parallels cellular automata and swarm intelligence. Simple local interactions produce complex global behavior — a principle fundamental to both game design and artificial life research.

Prototype Notes

Top-down scrolling world with objects at multiple size tiers. The katamari’s visual radius grows with each pickup. Objects visibly “stick” to the ball surface by orbiting it. Green glow indicates collectible objects; red outlines show objects that are still too large. Web Audio provides pickup pops that pitch-shift as the ball grows.
