SPT-010 · DARTS

SPT — Sports Prototype #010 ★★★★

How to Play

Play a game of 501 darts against an AI opponent. Both players start at 501 and must reach exactly zero, finishing on a double.

  • Mouse — Aim at the dartboard (crosshair follows with slight wobble)
  • Click + Hold — Charge throw power (green bar fills up)
  • Release — Throw the dart. Medium power is most accurate.
  • R — Start a new game
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Each turn consists of 3 darts. After your 3 darts, the AI takes its turn. If a throw would take your score below zero or to exactly 1 (impossible to finish on a double), the entire turn's score is voided ("bust").

History & Lineage

Darts evolved from medieval soldiers throwing shortened arrows at the bottom of wine barrels or cross-sections of tree trunks. The concentric rings of tree growth inspired the scoring zones. By the 1860s, the game had formalized in English pubs.

Brian Gamlin, a Lancashire carpenter, devised the modern numbering system in 1896. His genius was placing high numbers adjacent to low ones — 20 sits between 1 and 5, punishing inaccuracy. The standard "clock" board has survived unchanged for over 125 years.

Phil "The Power" Taylor dominated professional darts for two decades, winning 16 World Championships. The PDC (Professional Darts Corporation) transformed the sport into a televised spectacle with prize pools exceeding £2.5 million.

AI Archaeology

Era 5 — Statistical Learning

Darts is a masterclass in the explore-exploit tradeoff. Beginners should aim for the large 19 segment (surrounded by 7 and 3 — still decent scores), not triple-20. Only players with professional-level precision benefit from targeting T20, because near-misses land in 1 or 5.

Research by Ryan Tibshirani (Stanford) modeled optimal dart aiming as a function of throwing variance. The result: for most humans, the optimal target is significantly left of the triple-20, toward the triple-19 region. This mirrors the Kelly criterion in gambling — optimal strategy depends on your edge.

Motor control optimization in robotics faces the same challenge: balancing precision targets against the cost of missing. A robot arm throwing darts must learn its own noise profile and adapt its aim accordingly — exactly what human players do through thousands of hours of practice.

Classification

Precision aiming sport. Single-player vs AI. Turn-based with real-time throwing mechanic. 501 countdown scoring with double-out finish requirement.

Prototype Notes

Implements full PDC-standard dartboard geometry with 20 numbered segments, single/double/triple rings, and bullseye. AI opponent uses Gaussian-distributed aim with adjustable skill level. Power mechanic adds risk/reward — optimal power gives best accuracy, while under/over-powering scatters the throw.
