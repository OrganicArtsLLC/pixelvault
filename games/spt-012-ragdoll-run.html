SPT-012 · RAGDOLL RUN

SPT — Sports Prototype #012 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Run 100 meters using the most unintuitive controls ever designed. Good luck getting past 2 meters.

  • Q — Push left thigh forward, right thigh back
  • W — Push right thigh forward, left thigh back
  • O — Push left calf forward
  • P — Push right calf back
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tip: Try alternating Q and W rhythmically while timing O and P to straighten your legs. Or just mash keys and laugh at the result.

History

QWOP (2008) by Bennett Foddy became a viral sensation by turning the simple act of running into an absurdly difficult challenge. Rather than pressing "right" to run, players must independently control each muscle group of a sprinter's legs using Q, W, O, and P keys.

Foddy, a philosopher and game designer at NYU, explored the idea that difficulty itself can be the primary source of entertainment. The game spawned an entire genre of "deliberately awkward control" games including GIRP, Getting Over It with Bennett Foddy, and inspired titles like Surgeon Simulator and Octodad.

Most players never complete the 100-meter dash. The world record is under 50 seconds, achieved through a precisely timed key sequence that looks nothing like natural running.

AI Archaeology

Era 5-6 — Expert Systems to Transformer Era

QWOP presents a fascinating challenge for reinforcement learning. The state space is continuous (joint angles, velocities, body position), and the reward signal (distance traveled) is sparse and deceptive — many local optima involve crawling, knee-walking, or falling forward repeatedly.

Deep RL agents can eventually learn to "run" in QWOP, but their solutions rarely resemble human running. They typically discover exploit gaits — knee-shuffles or controlled falls — that maximize distance without achieving the intended bipedal locomotion. This mirrors the broader challenge of sim-to-real transfer in robotics.

Prototype Notes

  • Simplified 2D ragdoll with torso, two thighs, two calves, two feet
  • Spring-damper joints with angular limits simulate muscle resistance
  • Controls apply torques to joints — deliberately unintuitive mapping
  • Fall detection: head or torso touching ground ends the run
  • Distance markers on track provide sense of (minimal) progress
  • The challenge IS the game — most attempts end within 1 second
