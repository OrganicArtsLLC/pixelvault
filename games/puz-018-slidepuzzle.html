PUZ-018 · SLIDE PUZZLE

PUZ — Puzzle Prototype #018 ★★★★

How to Play

Slide numbered tiles in a 4×4 grid to arrange them in order from 1 to 15, left-to-right and top-to-bottom, with the empty space in the bottom-right corner.

  • Click a tile adjacent to the empty space to slide it
  • Arrow keys — slide the tile from that direction into the empty space
  • R — Shuffle the board
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Solve the top row first, then the second row, then the bottom two rows together
  • Work left-to-right within each row
  • The move counter tracks your efficiency — experts solve in under 80 moves

History & Lineage

Noyes Chapman invented the 15-puzzle in 1874. By 1880 it had become a worldwide craze, with newspapers publishing solutions and offering prizes. Sam Loyd, the famous puzzle-maker, claimed to have invented it and offered $1,000 for anyone who could solve a configuration with tiles 14 and 15 swapped — knowing it was mathematically impossible.

Mathematicians proved that exactly half of all possible arrangements are reachable from the solved state. The puzzle became a cornerstone of combinatorial mathematics and group theory, demonstrating permutation parity in a tangible, physical form.

AI Archaeology

Era 1–5 — Search & Symbols to Statistical (A* search, Manhattan distance, IDA*).

The 15-puzzle was one of the first problems tackled by AI search algorithms. The Manhattan distance heuristic — the sum of each tile's horizontal and vertical distance from its goal position — became a textbook example of an admissible heuristic for A* search. It never overestimates the true cost, guaranteeing optimal solutions.

IDA* (Iterative Deepening A*) by Richard Korf in 1985 solved random 15-puzzle instances optimally, proving the average optimal solution length is about 52.5 moves. The puzzle remains a standard benchmark for heuristic search research.

Prototype Notes

This prototype implements solvability checking via permutation inversion counting. Only even-parity shuffles are generated. Smooth tile animation uses linear interpolation. The beveled tile style references the physical wooden sliding puzzles of the 1880s.
