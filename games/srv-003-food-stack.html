SRV-003 · FOOD STACK

SRV — Service / Serve 'em Up Prototype #003 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Walk across burger ingredients on platforms to make them fall. Assemble complete burgers by dropping all parts to the bottom plates. Avoid the food enemies that chase you on platforms and ladders.

  • ← → — Walk left / right on platforms
  • ↑ ↓ — Climb up / down ladders
  • Space — Pepper spray (stuns nearby enemies, limited uses)
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Walk fully across: You must walk across the entire width of an ingredient to make it drop.
  • Chain reactions: When a falling ingredient lands on another, the lower one drops too. Chain drops score big.
  • Squish enemies: Dropping an ingredient onto an enemy squishes them for bonus points.
  • Pepper sparingly: You only have a few pepper shots. Use them to create escape routes.
  • Ladder strategy: Enemies follow you onto ladders. Use this to lure them away from ingredients.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: BurgerTime (1982, Data East) → Peter Pepper's Ice Cream Factory (1984) → BurgerTime Deluxe (1991, Game Boy)

BurgerTime (1982) was designed by Akira Fujita at Data East. The player controls Peter Pepper, a chef who must assemble hamburgers by walking over the ingredients laid across platforms. The game's innovation was its objective-based platforming: instead of reaching a goal, you must physically traverse specific tiles to complete a construction task.

The enemies — Mr. Hot Dog, Mr. Pickle, and Mr. Egg — chase using simple pursuit on the platform-and-ladder grid. Their movement is constrained to the same paths as the player, creating a shared navigation space where positional advantage matters more than speed.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (1980-1987)

Contemporary AI: Expert systems were dominant. MYCIN, DENDRAL. Path-planning algorithms were well-understood but rarely used in games due to hardware constraints.

Hidden Intelligence: BurgerTime's enemies use simple deterministic chase — move toward the player on the shared platform/ladder grid. But the multi-level structure creates emergent complexity. The player must plan paths that simultaneously complete the burger assembly objective AND manipulate enemy positions. This is a multi-objective planning problem: the state space includes both ingredient positions and enemy positions.

The Convergence: The ingredient-dropping mechanic is essentially a state-machine puzzle embedded in a real-time action game. Each ingredient has discrete states (its current platform level), and the player's traversal triggers state transitions. Modern task-planning AI solves similar problems — finding action sequences that achieve goal states while avoiding hazards.

If AI Had Designed This: Adaptive enemy routing that accounts for ingredient states — enemies would guard undropped ingredients more aggressively, creating a dynamic difficulty system tied to player progress.

Classification

Series: SRV — Service / Serve 'em Up

BurgerTime combines platforming with an assembly objective. The player serves food by constructing it — walking over ingredients to build burgers. The service mechanic is the core goal.

Core Mechanic

Objective-based traversal with assembly construction. Walk across ingredients to drop them. Multi-level platforms with ladder connections. Chase enemies on shared navigation grid. Pepper spray as limited defensive tool. Chain-reaction drops for scoring.
