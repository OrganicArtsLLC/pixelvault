How to Play

Play Sepak Takraw — Southeast Asian kick volleyball! Hit the rattan ball over the net using only your feet, knees, chest, and head. No hands allowed!

First player to 15 points wins! The ball must clear the net. If it lands on your side, your opponent scores. Bicycle kick animations trigger on high ball contact!

History

Sepak Takraw (literally "kick ball" in Malay-Thai) has roots stretching back to the 15th century Malacca Sultanate in Southeast Asia. The sport evolved from informal circle games (sepak raga) where players kept a woven rattan ball aloft using only their feet, knees, chest, and head.

The modern competitive net version was formalized in the 1960s when the Asian Sepak Takraw Federation (ASTAF) established standardized rules. It became a medal event at the Asian Games in 1990. The sport is most popular in Thailand, Malaysia, Indonesia, Myanmar, and the Philippines, where it is known by various names including chinlone, sipa, and da cau.

What sets sepak takraw apart from other net sports is the sheer athleticism required — players routinely perform bicycle kicks and spinning scissor kicks at heights exceeding the 1.52m net, creating one of the most spectacular displays in any sport.

AI Archaeology

Era 3 — Expert Systems

Early sports game AI relied on rule-based expert systems for positioning, shot selection, and difficulty scaling. Sepak takraw presents an interesting AI challenge: the constraint of no hand use means the AI must model acrobatic body positioning — deciding when to use a simple toe kick versus committing to a bicycle kick requires timing heuristics similar to fighting game AI.

Modern sports AI would use motion prediction and trajectory planning, but this prototype's rule-based opponent captures the spirit of 1990s sports game AI — competent but readable, with clear behavioral patterns the player can learn to exploit.
