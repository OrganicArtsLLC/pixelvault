PLT-005 · BARREL CLIMB

PLT — Platformer Prototype #005 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Climb to the top of the construction site to rescue Pauline. Dodge or jump over barrels thrown by Donkey Kong. Grab hammers to smash barrels for bonus points.

  • ← → — Move left / right
  • ↑ ↓ — Climb up / down ladders
  • Space — Jump
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Jump barrels: Time your jumps to clear rolling barrels for 100 points each.
  • Ladders: You can only climb when directly overlapping a ladder. Press up or down.
  • Hammers: Collect the flashing hammer to smash barrels for 300 points. The power wears off quickly.
  • Barrel paths: Barrels follow sloped girders and sometimes descend ladders randomly.
  • Reach Pauline: Get to the very top platform to clear the level. Each level is faster.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Space Panic (1980, Universal) → Donkey Kong (1981, Nintendo) → Mario Bros. (1983) → Super Mario Bros. (1985)

Donkey Kong (1981) was Shigeru Miyamoto's first major game design, created when Nintendo needed to repurpose unsold Radar Scope cabinets for the North American market. Miyamoto, a young industrial designer with no programming experience, conceived a narrative scenario — a gorilla kidnaps a carpenter's girlfriend — that gave the player a visible goal beyond high scores.

The game introduced "Jumpman" (later Mario), who became gaming's most recognizable character. The single-screen level design with sloped girders, ladders, and rolling barrels created a spatial puzzle where timing and pathfinding interleave. Each of the four original screens presented a distinct mechanical challenge.

Space Panic (1980) is often cited as the first platform game — it featured ladders and platforms but no jumping. Donkey Kong added the jump mechanic, cementing the platformer genre.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (1980-1987)

Contemporary AI: Expert systems boom. MYCIN, R1/XCON. Rule-based reasoning with goal structures.

Hidden Intelligence: Donkey Kong's design embodies goal-directed behavior — the player has a visible objective (Pauline at the top) and must plan a path through dynamic obstacles. This mirrors the shift from reactive AI (respond to stimuli) to planning-based AI (pursue goals through obstacle navigation). The barrel-throwing pattern creates a stochastic obstacle field requiring real-time path replanning.

The Convergence: The game's level structure — four distinct screens with escalating complexity — anticipates curriculum learning in modern ML. Each screen teaches a mechanic (dodging, hammering, elevators, rivets) before combining them. The barrel's random ladder-descent creates a partially observable planning problem.

If AI Had Designed This: Adaptive barrel-throw timing calibrated to player skill. Dynamic ladder placement that maximizes the number of "close call" moments. Difficulty curves shaped by reinforcement learning from player death data.

Classification

Series: PLT — Platformer

Donkey Kong is the archetypal single-screen platformer. Gravity, jumping, ladders, and rolling obstacles on sloped surfaces. Goal-directed traversal rather than score-chasing.

Core Mechanic

Climb-and-dodge on sloped girders with ladder traversal. Rolling barrels as dynamic obstacles. Jump for evasion and points. Hammer power-up for temporary offense. Single-screen level with visible goal.

What This Prototype Tests

  • Sloped girder physics — do barrels roll convincingly on angled platforms?
  • Ladder mechanics — does climbing feel responsive and fair?
  • Barrel timing — do barrel patterns create learnable-but-challenging rhythms?
  • Goal-directed flow — does the visible rescue objective create forward momentum?
  • Hammer power fantasy — does the temporary invincibility feel rewarding?

Prototype Notes

This is a sketch rated 4/5. Single-screen level with six sloped girders, ladders, barrel spawning from Donkey Kong at top. Hammer power-up on two platforms. Levels repeat with increasing barrel frequency.
