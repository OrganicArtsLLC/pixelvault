SIM-017 · UNBOX SORT

SIM — Simulation Prototype #017 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Click the cardboard box to pull out an item. Drag items to place them in the room. Each item has a natural home — books on shelves, clothes in the closet, dishes in the kitchen area.

  • Click the box to unpack the next item
  • Drag items to place them in the room
  • Items glow green when correctly placed
  • Items flash red if placed somewhere wrong
  • Place all items correctly to complete the room
  • R — Restart current room
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

There is no timer. No score. Just arrange things where they belong.

History

Unpacking was created by Witch Beam, a small studio in Brisbane, Australia, and released in 2021. Designed by Wren Brier, the game tells a wordless story of a woman's life through the objects she unpacks across eight moves spanning 1997–2018. It won the BAFTA for Narrative and was nominated for Game of the Year at multiple ceremonies.

The game belongs to a lineage of "anti-games" that reject traditional challenge in favour of contemplation — alongside titles like Proteus, Mountain, and A Short Hike. Its genius is in what it withholds: no text tells you the story. You piece it together from a diploma, a stuffed animal that appears in every move, and the changing size of the living space.

AI Archaeology

Unpacking is a masterclass in environmental storytelling — a technique that AI narrative systems are only beginning to approach. The game's "puzzle" is empathy: to solve it, you must think about where a person would naturally put their things. This requires common-sense spatial reasoning and cultural knowledge that remains challenging for AI.

The game also demonstrates procedural rhetoric through absence. What items disappear between moves? What new items appear? The narrative is encoded in inventory deltas — a data structure that AI could analyse but would struggle to imbue with emotional meaning.

Classification

Primary: Object Placement / Environmental Narrative

Secondary: Zen Puzzle, Life Simulation

Input: Mouse (drag and drop)

Complexity: Minimal — accessibility is the point

Prototype Notes

This prototype condenses the experience into three rooms representing childhood, college, and first apartment. Items are drawn as simple coloured icons with labels. The "correct zone" system approximates the original's spatial logic. The recurring teddy bear across all three stages mirrors the original's storytelling device.
