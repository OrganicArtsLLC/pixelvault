How to Play

Throw water bombs that burst into cascading waves! Water flows across platforms, pools in gaps, and sweeps enemies away.

Tips

  • Water flows downhill and pools in low spots — use terrain to your advantage!
  • Enemies swept by water are washed off the screen for bonus points.
  • You can throw bombs while jumping for aerial coverage.
  • Water cascades off platform edges — catch enemies below!
  • Clear all enemies to advance to the next level.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Liquid Kids / Mizubaku Adventure (1990, Taito) → Bubble Bobble (1986) → Snow Bros (1990)

Released by Taito in 1990 as “Mizubaku Adventure” in Japan, Liquid Kids featured Hipopo the hippo throwing water bombs at enemies. The game stood out for its novel water physics — bombs exploded into waves that flowed realistically across platforms, pooled in depressions, and cascaded off edges. This was remarkably ambitious for 1990 arcade hardware.

The game belongs to Taito’s lineage of cute single-screen platformers alongside Bubble Bobble and The New Zealand Story. While less well-known than its siblings, Liquid Kids’ water mechanics were ahead of their time, anticipating physics-driven combat that wouldn’t become mainstream until games like Lemmings and Worms.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion

Liquid Kids represents an early experiment in physics-as-gameplay. The water simulation — flowing, pooling, cascading — turned the environment into a dynamic weapon. Each water bomb created emergent outcomes depending on terrain topology. This “systemic combat” approach, where outcomes emerge from physics rules rather than scripted interactions, prefigures modern sandbox games. The water didn’t just damage enemies; it reshaped the tactical landscape of each level in real time.
