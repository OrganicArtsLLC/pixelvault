FGT-004 · MARTIAL RUN

How to Play

You are a martial artist storming a mountain fortress to rescue the princess. You alternate between two stances:

  • Running Stance — press Left/Right to move. You cannot attack or block, but you move fast.
  • Fighting Stance — press Z to punch (fast, short range) or X to kick (slower, longer range). Press Up/Down to aim high or low. Press direction matching an incoming attack to block.

Space toggles between stances. Approach enemies in running stance, then switch to fight. Defeat all 8 guards and the final boss Akuma to reach the princess.

Warning: If you approach the princess in fighting stance, she will kick you — just like the original game. Switch to running stance first!

History & Lineage

Karateka (1984) was created by Jordan Mechner for the Apple II while he was still a student at Yale. It was the first game to use rotoscoped animation — Mechner filmed his father (a karate practitioner) performing moves, then traced the frames pixel by pixel to achieve unprecedented fluidity of motion.

The game pioneered cinematic game design: no HUD during gameplay, dramatic camera angles, and a continuous scrolling approach to the fortress that told a story through movement alone. Mechner would later use the same rotoscoping technique to create Prince of Persia (1989).

The Way of the Exploding Fist (1985, Beam Software) expanded the fighting game vocabulary with multiple attack types and directional combat, influencing every fighting game that followed.

AI Archaeology

Karateka's rotoscoping pipeline — filming human motion, digitizing it frame by frame, and mapping it to game characters — was a manual precursor to modern motion capture. Today, ML-based pose estimation (OpenPose, MediaPipe) automates what Mechner did by hand: extracting skeletal keypoints from video to drive character animation.

The stance-switching mechanic is an early example of modal game AI design — the same inputs produce different outputs depending on state, a pattern now fundamental to state machines in game AI and robotics.

Prototype Notes

This prototype captures the core stance-toggle rhythm of the original. The cinematic interludes (gate, eagle) are simplified but preserve the feeling of progression through a fortress. The princess-kick easter egg honors one of gaming's earliest narrative punchlines. Combat uses a three-zone (high/mid/low) system for attacks and blocks.
