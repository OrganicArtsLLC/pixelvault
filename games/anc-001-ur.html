ANC-001 · ROYAL GAME OF UR

ANC — Ancient Games Prototype #001 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Race your 7 pieces around the board and off the end before your opponent. Roll tetrahedral dice (0-4), then choose which piece to move. Land on an opponent's piece to capture it (send it back to start). Rosette squares (marked) grant an extra turn and are safe from capture.

  • Space — Roll dice
  • 1-7 or click — Select which piece to move
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Rosettes: Landing on a rosette (star) gives an extra turn and protects from capture.
  • Shared track: The middle row is shared — this is where captures happen.
  • Blocking: You cannot land on your own pieces. Plan ahead.
  • Zero roll: Rolling 0 skips your turn.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Royal Game of Ur (~2600 BCE, Mesopotamia) → Senet (~3100 BCE) → Backgammon (~3000 BCE)

The Royal Game of Ur is the oldest playable board game with surviving rules, decoded by Irving Finkel at the British Museum from a cuneiform tablet. Boards were found in the Royal Tombs of Ur (modern Iraq), dating to approximately 2600 BCE.

The game combines luck (dice) with strategy (piece selection, blocking, capture). The shared middle track forces conflict — the same spatial resource creates both progress and danger. This is the ancestor of backgammon and all race-with-capture games.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 1 — Symbolic AI (pre-1965)

Contemporary AI: This game predates formal AI by 4600 years. But the mathematical structure — stochastic game with perfect information — is exactly what expectiminimax was designed to solve.

Hidden Intelligence: The Royal Game of Ur is a stochastic game with perfect information. The optimal algorithm is expectiminimax (minimax with chance nodes for dice). TD-Gammon (1992) solved backgammon — Ur's descendant — using temporal-difference learning, proving that ancient game designers had unknowingly created ideal RL training environments. The shared track creates a blocking game from the simplest possible spatial constraint.

The Convergence: 4600 years of humans playing this game generated the same behavioral patterns that RL agents discover: prioritize the shared track rosette, capture when possible, advance trailing pieces. Human intuition and machine optimization converge on the same strategies.

If AI Had Designed This: Self-play to discover the optimal track layout and number of pieces — the game might look different, but the core tension (shared space creates conflict) would remain.
