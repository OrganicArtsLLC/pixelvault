How to Play

Slam your folded paper card onto the ground next to your opponent's card to flip it over. Best of 5 rounds.

Tips

  • Distance matters: Slam close to the edge of the opponent's card for best flip chance.
  • Power balance: Too weak won't generate enough air pressure; too strong and your card bounces away.
  • Sweet spot: Aim for the near edge of the opponent's card — the air pocket underneath lifts it.
  • Watch the indicator: The arc shows your slam trajectory. Green zone = good flip probability.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Ddakji (딱지, traditional Korea) → Menko (Japan, Edo period) → Pogs (1990s)

Ddakji is a traditional Korean children's street game played with folded paper cards. Two thick square cards are made by interlocking two strips of paper or cardboard. Players take turns slamming their card onto the opponent's card resting on the ground. If the target card flips over from the impact and air pressure, the slammer wins that card.

The game became globally famous through its appearance in the first episode of Squid Game (2021), where the recruiter (Gong Yoo) challenges strangers to play ddakji for money. The scene perfectly captures the game's blend of skill, luck, and the visceral satisfaction of the slam.

Similar games exist across Asia: Japan's Menko uses thicker cardboard discs, and the 1990s Pog craze in the West used milk cap discs with a heavier slammer.

AI Archaeology

The flip probability in ddakji depends on a complex interplay of factors: slam distance from the target's edge, impact angle, card weight/stiffness, surface friction, and the air pocket created under the target card. An expert system can model the optimal slam parameters, but real-world paper physics introduces enough chaos that perfect play remains elusive. The AI opponent in this prototype uses a probability curve fitted to these parameters.
