How to Play

Shoot the centipede as it winds through the mushroom field. Each hit splits it into segments and spawns a new mushroom. Clear all segments to advance.

  • ← → ↑ ↓ or WASD — Move (in bottom zone)
  • Space — Shoot (auto-fire when held)
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Mushrooms redirect: The centipede turns down one row when it hits a mushroom or wall edge.
  • Splitting: Hitting a middle segment creates two independent centipedes and a mushroom.
  • Spider: A spider bounces around the bottom zone — dangerous but high-value target.
  • Clear mushrooms: Shoot mushrooms (3 hits) to create cleaner paths.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Centipede (1981, Dona Bailey/Ed Logg, Atari) → Millipede (1982, Atari)

Centipede was designed by Dona Bailey and Ed Logg at Atari — Bailey was one of the first women to design an arcade game. It introduced the trackball for fluid analog control and the concept of a battlefield that the player reshapes through play.

The mushroom field is the real design innovation. Every destroyed centipede segment leaves a mushroom, which changes future centipede paths. The player is simultaneously fighting enemies and sculpting the terrain they'll fight on next. This feedback loop creates emergent complexity from simple rules.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (1978–1983)

Contemporary AI: Rule-based systems. No concept of environment modification in AI decision-making.

Hidden Intelligence: The mushroom-spawning mechanic is implicit environment co-design. The player's actions reshape the obstacle field, which changes enemy behavior, which changes optimal player strategy. This is a closed-loop system where agent actions modify the environment's transition function — a concept RL researchers wouldn't formalize until reward shaping and environment design became subfields.

The Convergence: Modern sim-to-real transfer and domain randomization are about training agents in varying environments. Centipede's mushroom field is a player-modified environment that creates natural domain variation within a single game.

If AI Had Designed This: Procedurally optimized mushroom density to maintain the ideal tension between path predictability and chaos.

Classification

Series: FIX — Fixed Shooter

Player restricted to a zone, shooting upward at descending threats. Centipede extends the series with terrain modification and segmented enemies.

Core Mechanic

Trackball shooter with environment reshaping. Segmented enemy that splits on hit. Mushroom field as persistent, player-modified obstacle grid. Spider as secondary threat in player zone.

What This Prototype Tests

  • Spider pressure (does it create urgency in the player zone?)
