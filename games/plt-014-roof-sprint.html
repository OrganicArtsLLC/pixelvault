PLT — Platformer Prototype #014 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Run across the rooftops of a crumbling city. Your character runs automatically and keeps accelerating. You have one button: jump.

  • Space / Click — Jump
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Mechanics

  • Acceleration: You get faster over time. The longer you survive, the harder it gets.
  • Obstacles: Hit crates and antennas to slow down — sometimes this is a good thing.
  • Gaps: Miss a rooftop and you fall to your death.
  • Variable jump: Hold jump longer for higher jumps. Release early for short hops.

Tips

  • Speed management: Sometimes hitting an obstacle on purpose slows you to a safer speed.
  • Edge jumps: Jump at the last moment to clear wider gaps.
  • Watch the gaps: Buildings vary in height — be ready to land high or low.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Canabalt (2009, Adam Saltsman) ← Moon Patrol (1982, Irem) ← Pitfall! (1982, Activision)

Canabalt (2009) was created by Adam Saltsman in five days for the Experimental Gameplay Project. Built in Flash with the Flixel framework, it became a viral sensation and essentially invented the modern one-button endless runner genre.

Its genius lay in radical reduction: stripping away every control except jump, then making the environment do all the work. The procedural cityscape, the accelerating speed, the monochrome palette, the screen shake on landing — every element served the fantasy of a desperate rooftop escape.

The game inspired countless mobile runners including Temple Run, Jetpack Joyride, and the Chrome dinosaur game. Its influence proved that constraint breeds innovation: one button can create infinite depth when the world responds dynamically to the player’s single choice.

Classification

  • Primary: Platformer (endless horizontal runner)
  • Secondary: Procedural generation, reflexes, speed management
  • Input: Single button (jump)
  • Perspective: Side-on, horizontal auto-scroll

Prototype Notes

This prototype implements the core one-button auto-runner with procedural building generation, accelerating speed, obstacles that slow the player, parallax background layers, camera shake on landing, and atmospheric background events. The monochrome grey-scale palette captures the original’s stark aesthetic.
