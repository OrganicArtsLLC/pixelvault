How to Play

Two gorillas stand atop city skyscrapers, hurling exploding bananas at each other. Adjust your angle and velocity, account for wind, and try to hit your opponent!

  • ← / → — Adjust angle
  • ↑ / ↓ — Adjust velocity
  • Space — Fire banana
  • R — Restart game
  • ? — Toggle this info panel
  • Escape — Close info panel

Rules

  • Players take turns throwing bananas
  • Bananas follow a parabolic arc affected by gravity and wind
  • Hitting a building creates an explosion crater (destructible terrain)
  • Hit the opposing gorilla to score a point
  • First to 3 points wins the match
  • Wind direction and strength change each round
  • Player 2 is controlled by AI

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Gorillas (1991, IBM) ← Scorched Earth (1991) ← Artillery (1976, Mike Forman)

Gorillas shipped as GORILLA.BAS with QBasic on MS-DOS 5.0, making it one of the most widely distributed games in history. Written by IBM, it taught an entire generation of programmers by example — curious kids would read the BASIC source code, modify the gravity constant, change the gorilla sprites, and accidentally learn to program.

The artillery genre traces back to 1976's Artillery for the HP 2647A terminal. Scorched Earth (1991) expanded it into a full tactical game with weapons and terrain. But Gorillas, with its charming simplicity and universal distribution, remains the most beloved entry in the genre.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 2 — Expert Systems (1980s-1990s)

Contemporary AI: Expert systems dominated commercial AI. Rule-based reasoning could solve well-defined physics problems like ballistic trajectories, but struggled with the fuzzy spatial reasoning humans use intuitively.

Hidden Intelligence: Artillery games are inverse kinematics problems: given a target position, solve for launch angle and velocity under gravity and wind. The closed-form solution exists (it's high school physics), but the game's challenge comes from the human inability to compute it mentally. The AI opponent approximates the solution with deliberate noise injection — too perfect and it's unfun, too random and it's unchallenging.

The Convergence: Modern artillery game AI uses reinforcement learning to discover non-obvious strategies like deliberately destroying terrain to create better firing angles — emergent tactics that pure physics solvers miss.

Classification

Series: FIX (Fixed Shooter / Fixed Position) — gorillas are stationary on buildings

Core Mechanic: Parametric projectile physics (angle + velocity + wind)

Input: Keyboard — directional adjustment and fire

Prototype Notes

This sketch captures the essential Gorillas experience: the QBasic-era color palette, chunky pixel buildings, banana arc physics, and satisfying building destruction. The AI opponent calculates trajectories with human-like imperfection. Wind adds the crucial variable that prevents pure memorization. The victory dance is a love letter to the original's charm.
