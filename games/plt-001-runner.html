PLT-001 · RUNNER

PLT — Platform Prototype #001 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Run right, jump gaps, stomp enemies from above, collect coins, reach the flag.

  • ← → or A D — Run
  • Space or ↑ or W — Jump (hold for higher)
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Variable jump: Tap for short hop, hold for full height
  • Coyote time: You can still jump briefly after walking off an edge
  • Stomp: Land on enemies from above to defeat them. Touch from the side = death.
  • Falling into pits costs a life. Collect coins for score.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Donkey Kong (1981) → Super Mario Bros (1985) → Sonic the Hedgehog (1991)

The platformer is born from a simple question: what if jumping was the game? Donkey Kong (1981) gave players a character who could jump over obstacles. Super Mario Bros (1985) perfected the variable-height jump — hold the button longer, jump higher — which is still the gold standard for platformer feel. Sonic (1991) added momentum and speed as reward.

Mario's variable jump is the single most important design innovation in platform games. It turns one button into a continuous expression of intent. Every platformer since — from Celeste to Hollow Knight — inherits this mechanic.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (1978–1983)

Contemporary AI: Planning systems existed but couldn't handle real-time continuous control. No model-based RL. Discrete action spaces only — continuous control remained unsolved.

Hidden Intelligence: Variable-height jump (hold longer = jump higher) is continuous action control — the player chooses from an effectively infinite action space with every jump. This is inherently harder for AI than discrete actions, which is why platformers remained challenging long after Atari shooters were solved by DQN.

The Convergence: Level design in platformers is reward shaping — coins guide the player along intended paths, gaps punish deviation. Mario's levels are hand-crafted reward functions that teach through environmental design, exactly the principle behind curriculum learning and reward shaping in RL. Nintendo's level designers were building reward functions decades before the term existed.

If AI Had Designed This: Procedural level generation with emotional arcs — tension and release patterns mapped to specific player positions, formalizing what Mario Maker's community discovered empirically.

Classification

Series: PLT — Platform

Games where gravity is the primary antagonist. The player navigates vertically layered terrain through jumping, climbing, or falling. The core tension is spatial: can I reach that ledge?

Core Mechanic

Variable-height jump + scrolling world. The player fights gravity while navigating a world wider than the screen. Enemies and gaps create a rhythm of run-jump-stomp.

What This Prototype Tests

  • Variable-height jump feel (is the arc satisfying?)
  • Coyote time + jump buffering (forgiveness mechanics)
  • Enemy stomp as primary combat (risk/reward of jumping on things)
  • Horizontal scrolling camera (how much world context does the player need?)

Prototype Notes

This is a sketch — a mechanical skeleton, not a finished game. The goal is to feel the core mechanic from the builder's perspective, not to create a polished product.
