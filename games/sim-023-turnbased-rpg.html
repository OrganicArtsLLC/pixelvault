SIM — Simulation Prototype #023 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Command your party of three heroes across hex-grid battlefields in turn-based tactical combat. Move units, exploit terrain, and defeat all enemies to advance through four battle scenarios.

  • Click a friendly unit (blue) to select it
  • Click a highlighted hex to move the selected unit
  • Click an enemy (red highlight) in range to attack
  • Enter — End your turn
  • R — Restart current scenario
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Units

  • Knight (⚔) — Melee, 3 move, high HP. Your frontline tank.
  • Mage (★) — Ranged 2 hex, 2 move, AoE splash to adjacent hexes. Glass cannon.
  • Archer (↑) — Ranged 3 hex, 3 move, steady damage. Flexible positioning.

Terrain

  • Plains (green) — Normal movement, no bonus.
  • Forest (dark green) — +2 defense bonus. Good for holding positions.
  • Hills (brown) — +1 attack range for ranged units.
  • Water (blue) — Impassable. Plan routes around it.

Tips

  • Position your Knight in forests to absorb damage with the defense bonus.
  • Place ranged units on hills for extended reach.
  • Hex grids mean six neighbors — watch for flanking from unexpected angles.
  • Focus fire on one enemy at a time to reduce incoming damage quickly.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Astonishia Story (1994, Sonnori) — Korea’s first original RPG

Astonishia Story (어스토니시아 스토리) was released in 1994 by Sonnori, making it Korea’s first domestically developed RPG. At a time when Korean gamers played almost exclusively Japanese RPGs, Sonnori’s Lee Kwang-jin and his small team created a hex-grid SRPG that proved Korean developers could build world-class role-playing games.

The game featured hex-based tactical combat, a rarity even among Japanese SRPGs of the era, which gave it a distinctive tactical feel. Its success launched Sonnori as Korea’s first real RPG studio and helped establish a Korean RPG tradition that eventually led to games like The War of Genesis and Magna Carta.

The hex grid was a deliberate design choice — six-directional movement creates more nuanced positioning than the four-directional square grids used by Fire Emblem and Shining Force, the dominant SRPGs of that period.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Expert Systems (1980-1990)

The tactical AI in Astonishia Story used rule-based expert system approaches: evaluate terrain bonuses, assess threat levels, prioritize weakened targets, and maintain formation coherence. These heuristic rules encode tactical knowledge that human players develop through experience.

Hex grids expand the AI’s decision space significantly — each unit has six possible movement directions instead of four, and the staggered rows create asymmetric distance relationships that make pathfinding and threat evaluation more complex. This computational challenge is what makes hex-grid tactics games feel deeper and less predictable.

Prototype Notes

This prototype recreates the core hex-grid tactical combat of Astonishia Story across four escalating battle scenarios. The hex grid is the star — it creates six-directional adjacency that makes flanking, positioning, and terrain exploitation fundamentally richer than square-grid tactics. Three unit types (Knight/Mage/Archer) interact with four terrain types to create meaningful tactical decisions despite the small roster. The Korean SRPG tradition started here — Sonnori proved that Korean developers could create compelling RPGs, opening a door that an entire industry walked through.
