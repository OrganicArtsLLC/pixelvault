How to Play

Keep the jegi (제기) in the air by kicking it at the right moment! The jegi is a traditional Korean shuttlecock made from a coin wrapped in paper with colorful tassels.

  • ← → — Move your foot left / right
  • Space or Click — Kick the jegi
  • R — Restart game

Time your kick when the jegi is near your foot. Good timing sends it high; bad timing gives a weak kick. Miss entirely and the jegi hits the ground — game over! Build consecutive kicks for combo multipliers and watch the jegi trail grow more colorful.

History

Jegichagi (제기차기) is a traditional Korean game with roots stretching back centuries, particularly associated with Seollal (설날, Lunar New Year) celebrations. Players kick a jegi — a small shuttlecock made by wrapping a coin (traditionally a brass yeop-jeon) in mulberry paper (hanji) and cutting the paper into tassels.

The game has several traditional variants: ttanggangi (땅강이, kicking once then catching), heollaengi (헐랭이, continuous keepie-uppie), and competitive forms where players count consecutive kicks. Similar games exist worldwide — shuttlecock kicking (jianzi, 毽子) in China, sepak raga in Southeast Asia, and footbag/hacky sack in the West.

In Korea, jegichagi was traditionally a boys' winter pastime, played in courtyards and alleys. The game experienced a revival in the 1980s-90s as part of cultural heritage preservation efforts, and remains a beloved folk game taught in Korean schools.

AI Archaeology

Era 2 — Early Game AI

Jegichagi as a game design problem is deceptively simple: gravity, timing, and positioning. But the emergent depth comes from the physics — the jegi's slight lateral drift means the player must constantly reposition, creating a feedback loop between spatial awareness and rhythmic timing. The meditative quality of sustained play mirrors flow states studied in cognitive science.

From an AI perspective, this is a continuous control problem reducible to a 1D timing task with drift compensation. A simple PD controller could play perfectly, but the human challenge lies in the imprecision of motor control under time pressure — the same reason juggling is hard despite being mathematically trivial.
