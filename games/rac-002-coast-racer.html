How to Play

Race your Ferrari across branching roads, dodging traffic and roadside obstacles before time runs out. Choose your path at each fork.

Tips

  • Checkpoints: Reach each checkpoint before time expires to continue.
  • Forks: At the end of each stage the road splits — steer left or right to choose your path.
  • Traffic: Hitting other cars slows you dramatically.
  • Off-road: Driving on grass slows you down. Hitting roadside objects causes a crash.
  • Speed: Top speed is over 200 MPH — stay on the road and avoid traffic.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Out Run (1986, Yu Suzuki, Sega) ← Pole Position (1982, Namco)

Out Run (1986) was designed by Yu Suzuki at Sega and debuted in a hydraulic-cabinet arcade machine that tilted the player physically during turns. It was not a traditional racer — there was no lap, no competitors to beat. Instead, the player raced against time through branching landscapes, choosing paths at forks. This gave the player agency in a genre that had been purely linear.

The game's cultural impact was enormous: the Ferrari Testarossa, the radio-selectable soundtrack (Magical Sound Shower, Passing Breeze, Splash Wave), and the sun-drenched California aesthetic defined an entire era of arcade gaming. Suzuki called it a "driving game" rather than a "racing game" — it was about the joy of movement, not competition.

Pole Position (1982, Namco) pioneered the pseudo-3D racing perspective that Out Run perfected. The technique of scaling road segments to simulate depth became the standard approach for two decades of racing games.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (1978-1988)

The Scanline Trick: Out Run's road is not 3D. Each horizontal line of the screen represents a road segment at a different depth. The road is built from hundreds of horizontal strips, each slightly narrower than the one below it, creating the illusion of perspective. Curves are achieved by shifting each strip horizontally relative to the one below. Hills shift strips vertically.

Projection as Intelligence: This scanline-based projection is structurally identical to the projection operations used in early computer vision systems. The transformation from world coordinates to screen coordinates — scaling by inverse depth, horizontal offset by accumulated curvature — is the same math that pinhole camera models use. The game engine is, in a real sense, a reverse camera: instead of projecting a 3D world onto a 2D sensor, it projects a 1D road description into a 2D image that the human brain reconstructs as 3D.

Branching as Decision Tree: The fork structure creates a binary decision tree. With 3 branching points, there are 4 possible endings — a structure familiar to anyone who has studied game trees or decision processes. Each path leads to different scenery, difficulty, and experience.

Prototype Notes

This prototype implements the core pseudo-3D road rendering with segment-based projection, road curvature, hills, branching paths, traffic avoidance, roadside scenery, and a checkpoint timer system. Five stages are connected by three fork points, creating four possible routes to the finish. Each stage has a distinct color theme representing different landscapes.

The road rendering uses the classic scanline approach: each segment is projected based on its depth, with horizontal offsets accumulating to create curves and vertical offsets creating hills. Roadside objects (palm trees, signs, rocks) are scaled by depth and clipped to the road surface.
