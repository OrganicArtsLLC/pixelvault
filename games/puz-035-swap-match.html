PUZ-035 · SWAP MATCH

PUZ — Puzzle Prototype #035 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Swap adjacent blocks horizontally to form matches of 3 or more same-colored blocks in a row or column. Cleared blocks cause blocks above to fall, potentially creating chain combos for massive scores.

  • ← → ↑ ↓ — Move cursor
  • Space — Swap the two highlighted blocks
  • R — Restart game
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Scoring

  • Match 3 = 30 pts, Match 4 = 60 pts, Match 5 = 150 pts, Match 6 = 360 pts
  • Chain x2 = double, x3 = triple, etc.
  • Speed increases as your score rises

Tips

  • Set up blocks so that clearing one match causes blocks to fall into another match — that is a chain
  • The cursor can move during block falls — use this to extend chains
  • Watch the rising floor — don't let blocks reach the top row
  • Longer matches and higher chains earn dramatically more points

History

Panel de Pon was developed by Intelligent Systems and published by Nintendo in 1995 for the Super Famicom. In the West, it was rebranded as Tetris Attack with Yoshi characters, despite having no connection to Tetris. The core swap-match mechanic was revolutionary — instead of controlling falling pieces, players manipulated blocks already on the playfield.

The game became a competitive staple due to its chain combo system, which rewarded both pattern recognition and speed. Skilled players could set up elaborate multi-chain reactions that cleared huge portions of the board. Sequels appeared on N64 (Pokemon Puzzle League), GBA (Panel de Pon), and DS (Planet Puzzle League).

AI Insight

Panel de Pon's chain system creates emergent complexity — setting up multi-chain combos requires spatial prediction and timing that rewards both human intuition and AI lookahead. The rising floor adds constant pressure that transforms a matching puzzle into a real-time strategy game. Modern AI can find optimal chain setups, but human players compete through speed and pattern recognition intuition.

Prototype Notes

This prototype captures the core swap-match-chain loop on a 6-wide by 12-tall grid. Six distinct block colors with icon patterns. Continuous rising floor with increasing speed. Chain detection and combo scoring. The cursor moves freely during falls, enabling skilled chain extension.
