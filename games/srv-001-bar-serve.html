SRV-001 · BAR SERVE

SRV — Service / Dispatch Prototype #001 ★★★★☆

How to Play

You're the bartender. Customers march in from the right — slide them a beer before they reach you. Catch the empty mugs they slide back or you lose a life. If a customer reaches you, you're thrown out.

  • ↑ ↓ or W S — Move between bar rails
  • Space or Enter — Serve beer (slides right)
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Priority scan: Glance all 4 rails — serve the customer closest to you first.
  • Catch rhythm: After serving, watch for the return mug on that same rail.
  • Don't over-serve: A second beer collides with a returning mug — both are wasted.
  • Tipping: Each wave sends faster customers — there's no ceiling. Survive as long as possible.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Tapper (1983, Bally Midway) → Root Beer Tapper (1983) → Diner Dash (2004)

Tapper (1983) was designed by Larry Demar and Steve Ritchie at Bally Midway and originally sponsored by Anheuser-Busch (Budweiser branding). The game was so convincingly bar-themed that Midway re-released it as Root Beer Tapper for family arcades. It remains one of the few arcade games to use a tap handle as the control — an authentic beer tap mounted on the cabinet.

The game was unusual for its era: no shooting, no enemies with weapons. The threat is momentum and timing. Customers march relentlessly; the rules never change; the difficulty is pure throughput. It's the most honest representation of the service industry in arcade history.

The genre it founded — multi-lane timing service games — lay dormant for 20 years until Diner Dash (2004) revived it for casual audiences, spawning an entire category of "time management" games.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (1980–1997)

Contemporary AI: Operations research (OR) dominated service queue optimization. M/M/N queueing theory (Erlang, 1909 — re-popularized 1970s) described exactly this problem mathematically. AI systems in 1983 had no tools for real-time adaptive scheduling in physical contexts.

Hidden Intelligence: Tapper is a real-time multi-queue scheduling problem. The player's cognitive process — scan all queues, assess urgency, dispatch, switch, catch return — mirrors the Shortest Job First (SJF) and Round Robin scheduling algorithms that governed every computer OS in 1983. John Miller's bartender was running the same decision algorithm as the Intel 8086 task switcher.

The Convergence: Erlang's queueing theory (1909, revived 1950s–70s) modeled telephone exchanges as stochastic queues with service rates and arrival rates — exactly the Tapper problem. Neither Bally Midway nor the operations research community made the connection. They solved the same problem for different audiences.

If AI Had Designed This: A 1983 expert system could have added urgency indicators — coloring customers red as their "patience" depleted — giving the player the equivalent of a priority queue visualization. The game intuited this later in Diner Dash.

Classification

Series: SRV — Service / Dispatch

Games where the player manages delivery or dispatch across multiple simultaneous channels. Objects arrive with deadlines; the player routes responses; returns must be caught. The core tension: N queues compete for one server's attention at all times.

Core Mechanic

Multi-lane serve and catch. 4 rails, customers advance left, player launches mugs right, catches empties left. Customer collision and mug escape are symmetric loss conditions.
