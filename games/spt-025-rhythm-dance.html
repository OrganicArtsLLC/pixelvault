How to Play

A dance key combo rhythm game inspired by Audition Online. Memorize the arrow combo, input it, then press Space on the beat!

  • ← ↑ → ↓ — Input arrow combo sequence
  • Space — Confirm combo on the beat
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Watch the combo sequence displayed on screen. Type the full arrow sequence, then hit Space when the timing bar reaches the beat zone. Better timing = higher score. Combo length increases each round (4 to 8 arrows).

History

Audition Online (2004) by T3 Entertainment became Korea's most popular casual online game, defining the "casual dance game" genre for PC. Unlike arcade rhythm games like DDR where players react to scrolling arrows in real-time, Audition separates the challenge into two phases: memorize a key combo, then execute it and hit Space on the beat.

The game spread across Southeast Asia, Latin America, and China, becoming a cultural phenomenon with over 300 million registered accounts worldwide. Its social features — dance battles, avatar customization, in-game weddings — made it as much a social platform as a game. The franchise spawned mobile versions, sequels (Audition 2, Club Audition), and countless imitators.

The two-phase design (memorize then execute) was brilliant for online play — it reduced the need for frame-perfect input timing that arcade games demanded, making it accessible on variable-latency internet connections while still rewarding skill.

AI Archaeology

Era 5 — Statistical Learning

Audition's two-phase gameplay mirrors sequence-to-sequence models in machine learning: first encode (memorize the combo), then decode (execute at the right time). The combo length progression from 4 to 8 arrows directly tests working memory capacity — George Miller's famous "7 plus or minus 2" limit. An AI agent would need both sequence memory and precise temporal calibration, combining the challenges of sequence prediction with beat tracking.

The scoring system (timing accuracy x combo length) creates an interesting optimization problem: longer combos are worth more but harder to remember, creating a risk-reward curve that parallels exploration-exploitation tradeoffs in reinforcement learning.

Prototype Notes

This prototype captures Audition's core two-phase loop: memorize the combo, input it, then hit the beat. The character performs dance animations on success and stumbles on failure. BPM indicator and timing bar visualize the beat. Combo length scales from 4 to 8 arrows across rounds.

Simplifications: single-player only (original was multiplayer dance battle), no avatar customization, no song selection. The beat is synthesized rather than music-driven.
