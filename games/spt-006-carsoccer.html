How to Play

Drive your car into the ball to knock it into the opponent's goal. First to 5 goals wins!

  • ↑ — Accelerate forward
  • ↓ — Brake / Reverse
  • ← → — Steer left / right
  • Shift — Boost (uses boost meter)
  • R — Restart match
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Drive over glowing boost pads on the field to refill your boost meter faster.

History

Car soccer traces back to Soccar (later Supersonic Acrobatic Rocket-Powered Battle-Cars, 2008) by Psyonix, which evolved into Rocket League (2015) — one of the most successful esports titles ever. The concept of vehicular ball sports appeared earlier in games like Excite Truck's soccer mode and various Micro Machines minigames.

Rocket League proved that physics-based emergent gameplay could sustain a competitive scene, with its skill ceiling growing as players discovered aerial play, wave dashes, and flip resets — none of which were explicitly designed.

AI Archaeology

Era 6 — Transformer Era

Rocket League's AI challenge is extraordinary: agents must predict ball trajectories, plan car positioning in 3D, manage boost resources, and coordinate with teammates — all in real-time continuous control space. OpenAI and others have trained deep RL agents that achieve superhuman play, but the emergent mechanical skill (aerials, dribbles) required years of training compute.

The car-ball interaction creates a control problem so rich that even deep RL agents struggle to master aerial play. This prototype's top-down 2D simplification removes the aerial dimension but preserves the core physics interaction puzzle.

Prototype Notes

  • Top-down birds-eye view simplifies Rocket League to 2D plane
  • Tank-style steering: turn rate scales with speed for weighty feel
  • Ball physics: elastic collision with momentum transfer from car velocity
  • AI uses simple chase-and-position behavior — beatable but competitive
  • Boost pads on field recharge meter; strategic resource management
