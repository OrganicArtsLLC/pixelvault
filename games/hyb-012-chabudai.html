HYB-012 · CHABUDAI GAESHI

HYB — Hybrid Prototype #012 ★★★★☆

How to Play

You are seated at a family dinner table. Endure the frustrations building around you.

  • Your RAGE METER fills automatically as annoying things happen
  • When rage reaches 100%, press Space to FLIP THE TABLE
  • Timing matters — flip at peak rage pulse for maximum power
  • Objects fly with physics: plates, cups, food, even family members
  • Score = total distance of all objects + bonus for broken items
  • 5 scenarios with escalating absurdity
  • R — Restart | ? — Info panel

History

Cho Chabudai Gaeshi (“Super Table Flip”) is a 2009 arcade game by Taito, released exclusively in Japan. The arcade cabinet featured a physical miniature table edge that players would slam upward to flip. The game captures the Japanese cultural concept of “chabudai gaeshi” — flipping the dinner table in a fit of rage — an act associated with the stereotypical strict Japanese father.

The game was a surprise arcade hit, spawning sequels and becoming a cultural phenomenon. It demonstrated that games could be built entirely around a single moment of cathartic release.

AI Archaeology

Emotion as Mechanic

Most games reward skill improvement. Chabudai Gaeshi rewards emotional buildup and release. The frustration phase is deliberately designed to be annoying — the game is engineering your emotional state. This “affect loop” (frustration → catharsis) predates modern game design discussions about emotional engagement by years.

Physical Interface as Expression

The original arcade cabinet’s physical table was crucial — the act of slamming a real object channeled genuine physical release. This prototype substitutes timing-based input, but the emotional arc remains.

Prototype Notes

Five scenarios recreate escalating dinner frustrations. Physics simulation handles post-flip object trajectories with gravity, bounce, and breakage. The rage meter auto-fills but pulses at 100% for timing skill. Web Audio provides slurping, phone rings, and satisfying crash sounds.
