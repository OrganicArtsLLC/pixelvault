SIM-003 · FRONTIER TREK

SIM — Simulation Prototype #003 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Lead your party of five from Independence, Missouri to Oregon City. Manage supplies, health, and pace across 2000 miles of frontier.

  • 1-5 — Select menu choices
  • Space — Continue / advance
  • H — Hunt for food (costs bullets)
  • R — Restart after game over
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Pace: Grueling is fast but drains health. Steady is safest.
  • Food: Hunt often. Starvation kills quickly.
  • Rivers: Fording is free but risky when deep. Ferry is safe but costs money.
  • Forts: Buy supplies when you can afford them.
  • Party: Once someone dies, they're gone. Keep health above zero.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: The Oregon Trail (1971, Don Rawitsch) → MECC Apple II version (1985) → Oregon Trail II (1995)

Originally created by student teacher Don Rawitsch in 1971 as a text-only teletype game. MECC's 1985 Apple II version became the definitive educational game of a generation. Its resource management, random events, and decision-making under uncertainty taught millions of children about pioneer life.

"You have died of dysentery" became one of gaming's most iconic phrases, illustrating how the game's random event system could create memorable narratives from simple probability tables.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (1980–1990)

Contemporary AI: Early Monte Carlo methods and simulation modeling were being applied to operations research and logistics.

Hidden Intelligence: Oregon Trail's event system is a Monte Carlo simulation. Each day, the game samples from probability distributions for weather, disease, accidents, and encounters. Resource depletion follows deterministic curves modified by stochastic events. The player makes decisions under uncertainty with incomplete information — a multi-armed bandit problem where each choice (pace, rations, route) has hidden payoff distributions.

The Convergence: The game's journey structure maps directly to sequential decision processes. Each landmark is a state, each choice is an action, and survival probability is the reward signal. This is a Markov Decision Process before the term was common in game design.

If AI Had Designed This: Adaptive difficulty — adjusting event probabilities based on current resources to maintain tension. A reinforcement learning agent could learn optimal supply ratios and pacing strategies.

Classification

Series: SIM — Simulation

Oregon Trail is the foundational resource-management journey simulation. All subsequent "manage supplies across a journey" games trace lineage here.

Core Mechanic

Resource management under stochastic events along a fixed route. Buy supplies, set pace, hunt for food, make river crossing decisions, survive random events. Party health as aggregate survival metric.

What This Prototype Tests

  • Resource tension — do supply decisions feel consequential?
  • Event variety — does each journey feel different?
  • Pace trade-off — is the speed-vs-health decision meaningful?
  • Hunting — does the mini-game break up the text simulation?

Prototype Notes

This is a sketch rated 4/5. Text-heavy simulation rendered entirely on canvas. Hunting mini-game provides action breaks. Random events sampled from probability tables. Five party members with individual health tracking.
