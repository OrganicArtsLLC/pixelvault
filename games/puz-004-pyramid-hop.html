PUZ-004 · PYRAMID HOP

PUZ — Puzzle Prototype #004 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Hop across the isometric pyramid and change every cube to the target color. Avoid enemies and don't fall off the edges!

  • ↑ — Hop up-right (diagonal)
  • ↓ — Hop down-left (diagonal)
  • ← — Hop up-left (diagonal)
  • → — Hop down-right (diagonal)
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Plan your path: Try to color cubes in efficient sweeps rather than random hopping.
  • Flying discs: Jump toward a disc on the edge to teleport back to the top. Coily will follow you off the edge and die.
  • Coily: The purple snake bounces down the pyramid as an egg, then hatches and chases you. Lure him off the edge or use a disc.
  • Slick & Sam: Green and blue creatures that revert tile colors. Catch them for bonus points before they undo your work.
  • Red balls: Bounce down randomly. Just stay out of their way.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Q*bert (1982, Gottlieb) → Marble Madness (1984, Atari) → modern isometric puzzlers

Q*bert was designed by Warren Davis and Jeff Lee at Gottlieb in 1982. The game's isometric pyramid was revolutionary — it presented a 2D grid as a 3D structure, making simple adjacency movement feel spatial and complex. The character's iconic "@!#?@!" speech balloon became one of gaming's most recognizable visual gags.

The game introduced multiple enemy types with distinct behaviors on the same topology: Coily chases, red balls fall randomly, Slick and Sam revert your progress. Each enemy creates a different kind of threat on the identical grid structure — a design insight that wouldn't be formalized until game design theory decades later.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (1980-1987)

Contemporary AI: Graph search, planning under constraints. The era of A* and spatial reasoning research.

Hidden Intelligence: Q*bert's core challenge is a spatial coverage problem — visit every node in a graph while avoiding mobile threats. This is structurally identical to the graph traversal and Hamiltonian path problems that dominated 1980s AI planning research. The isometric presentation disguises what is essentially a triangular grid coverage task.

The Convergence: Each enemy type transforms the same graph into a different planning problem. Coily makes it adversarial pursuit-evasion. Red balls make it stochastic obstacle avoidance. Slick and Sam make it a progress-preservation problem. The player must solve all three simultaneously on the same topology.

If AI Had Designed This: Adaptive enemy spawn rates calibrated to the player's coverage efficiency. Coily's pathfinding would use the same A* the player implicitly uses, creating a true adversarial search problem on an isometric graph.

Classification

Series: PUZ — Puzzle

Q*bert combines spatial coverage with enemy avoidance on an isometric grid. The puzzle is path planning; the action is timing and evasion.

Core Mechanic

Isometric tile-coloring with multi-threat avoidance. Triangular grid as 3D pyramid. Diagonal-only movement. Color every tile. Multiple enemy behaviors on shared topology. Flying discs as escape mechanism.

What This Prototype Tests

  • Isometric rendering — does the pyramid read as 3D?
  • Diagonal controls — does arrow-key-to-diagonal mapping feel intuitive?
  • Coverage pressure — does coloring all tiles feel like a satisfying puzzle?
  • Enemy variety — do different enemy types create distinct threats on the same grid?

Prototype Notes

This is a sketch rated 4/5. Seven-row isometric pyramid with 3D cube rendering. Hop animation between cubes. Coily, red balls, and Slick/Sam enemies. Flying discs on pyramid edges. Level progression increases enemy frequency and may require multiple color changes per tile in later levels.
