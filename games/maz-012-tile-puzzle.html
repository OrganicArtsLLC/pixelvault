How to Play

Guide Chip through tile-based puzzle levels. Collect all computer chips to open the exit socket, then reach the exit portal to advance.

  • ↑ ↓ ← → — Move Chip one tile at a time
  • Collect colored keys to open matching locked doors
  • Collect all computer chips to open the chip socket (exit gate)
  • Push movable blocks into water to create a path
  • Ice tiles slide you until you hit a wall; force floors push you in their arrow direction
  • Water is deadly without flippers; fire is deadly without fire boots
  • Avoid patrolling enemies — they are lethal on contact
  • R — Restart current level

History

Chip's Challenge was created by Chuck Sommerville for the Atari Lynx in 1989, originally as a showcase for the handheld's capabilities. The game achieved legendary status when Microsoft bundled it with the Windows Entertainment Pack in 1992, introducing millions of PC users to its 149 levels of escalating puzzle complexity. Each level teaches a new concept through play, building from simple key-and-door puzzles to devious multi-step challenges involving ice slides, force floors, and enemy choreography. The game inspired a devoted community that continues to create custom levels decades later.

AI Archaeology

AI Era 3 — Expert Systems

Chip's Challenge exemplifies how deterministic tile interaction rules — each tile type has exactly one behavior — combine to create emergent puzzle complexity. This compositional approach mirrors expert system design, where small, clear rule bases generate rich, sometimes surprising behavior. Solving levels optimally requires the kind of forward planning and constraint satisfaction that early AI research explored extensively.

Prototype Notes

Six hand-crafted levels introduce tile types progressively: keys and doors, computer chips, dirt paths, ice slides, force floors, water and blocks, fire and boots, and patrolling enemies. Each level is designed to teach a concept before combining them. The viewport scrolls to follow Chip through maps larger than the screen. Grid-based movement with one tile per keypress preserves the original's deliberate, puzzle-focused feel.
