FGT-002 · IRON FIST

FGT — Fighter Prototype #002 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Fight your way through five floors of a building, punching and kicking waves of enemies. Clear all enemies and defeat the boss on each floor to advance.

  • ← → — Move left/right
  • Z — Punch (fast, short range, 100 pts)
  • X — Kick (slower, longer range, 200 pts)
  • Space — Jump (combine with Z/X for jump attacks)
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Grippers: They grab and hold you, draining health. Mash left/right to shake them off.
  • Knife Throwers: They throw projectiles from range. Duck or jump over knives, then close in.
  • Tom Toms: Small and fast. They rush in low — use kicks for extra range.
  • Bosses: Each floor ends with a boss that takes multiple hits. Watch their patterns.
  • Timer: Each floor has a countdown. Run out and you lose a life.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Kung-Fu Master (1984, Irem/Takashi Nishiyama) → Double Dragon (1987) → Final Fight (1989) → Streets of Rage (1991)

Kung-Fu Master (1984), designed by Takashi Nishiyama for Irem, is widely considered the first beat-em-up. Based on the Bruce Lee film "Game of Death," the player ascends a five-story building fighting waves of enemies on each floor. The formula — walk right, fight enemies, beat the boss — became the template for every beat-em-up that followed.

Nishiyama would later join Capcom and create Street Fighter (1987), making him arguably the single most influential figure in fighting game history. The walk-and-fight loop he established in Kung-Fu Master is the direct ancestor of both the beat-em-up and fighting game genres.

Karate Champ (1984, Data East) approached martial arts from the competitive angle, establishing one-on-one fighting. Together, these two 1984 games laid the foundation for all fighting games.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (1978–1983, influence through 1984)

Contemporary AI: Expert systems were dominant. Rule-based production systems at their peak.

Hidden Intelligence: Kung-Fu Master's wave spawning system is a simple but effective form of procedural difficulty. Enemies spawn from both screen edges with increasing frequency and variety as floors progress. The spawn timing follows simple rules — frequency increases, pauses between waves decrease, enemy mix shifts toward harder types. This is an early form of dynamic difficulty adjustment, predating the concept by decades.

The Convergence: The floor-by-floor structure creates a natural curriculum — each floor introduces new enemy types and combinations, effectively training the player. Modern AI training uses curriculum learning (easy tasks first, then harder ones) to achieve better results. Nishiyama intuited this in 1984.

If AI Had Designed This: Adaptive spawn rates calibrated to player skill. If the player is taking damage, spawn fewer enemies. If the player is dominating, increase spawn rate and mix in harder types. Modern dynamic difficulty systems do exactly this.

Classification

Series: FGT — Fighter / Combat

The FGT series covers games where combat is the primary mechanic. Kung-Fu Master establishes the walk-and-fight beat-em-up template that would define the genre for decades.

Core Mechanic

Side-scrolling beat-em-up with wave-based enemy spawning. Punch and kick through corridors of enemies. Floor-clearing structure with boss fights. Health bar combat (not instant death). Timer pressure.
