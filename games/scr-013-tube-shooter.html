SCR-013 · TUBE SHOOTER

SCR — Scrolling Shooter Prototype #013 ★★★★☆

How to Play

You are a claw-shaped blaster sitting on the rim of a geometric tube. Enemies emerge from the center and climb toward you. Shoot them before they reach the rim!

  • ← → or A D — Move along the rim
  • Space or Z — Shoot into the tube
  • R — Restart after game over
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Flippers: Climb lanes and flip between adjacent lanes at the rim. Kill them before they reach you.
  • Tankers: Split into two Flippers when destroyed. Clear the lane quickly.
  • Spikers: Leave spikes in lanes. Spikes kill you during the descent at level end — avoid spiked lanes.
  • Descent: After clearing all enemies, you descend into the tube. Avoid lanes with spikes!
  • Tube shapes: Each level has a different geometric shape. The shape changes the tactical landscape.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Tempest (1981, Dave Theurer, Atari)

Tempest was designed by Dave Theurer, who also created Missile Command. The game originated from a nightmare Theurer had about monsters climbing out of a hole in the ground. Originally conceived as a first-person version of Space Invaders looking down a tube, the design evolved into something far more original.

Tempest was one of the first games to use Atari's Color-QuadScan vector display, producing vivid color vector graphics. The tube-rim perspective was revolutionary — a pseudo-3D viewpoint years before true 3D became common. The spinner controller allowed fluid analog movement around the rim.

The game introduced the concept of "zapping" — clearing a level by destroying all enemies, then descending through the tube to the next shape. Jeff Minter's Tempest 2000 (1994, Jaguar) later revitalized the franchise with techno music and particle effects.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 2 — Discrete Logic (1971–1977 influence, hardware-era design)

Contemporary AI: Early neural network research existed but game AI relied on discrete state logic and hardware-driven behavior.

Hidden Intelligence: Tempest's lane system is a brilliant spatial discretization. The continuous tube surface is quantized into segments (lanes), reducing player movement to a 1D circular array. Enemy AI is lane-aware: Flippers climb their lane then pathfind to the player's lane at the rim. This creates a discrete graph problem where enemies navigate a simple topology.

The Convergence: Modern navigation meshes in 3D games use the same principle — discretize continuous space into traversable regions. Tempest's lanes are essentially a 1D navmesh. The Flipper's lane-switching behavior at the rim is a primitive form of last-mile pathfinding.

If AI Had Designed This: Dynamic tube deformation based on player skill. Enemy spawn patterns would adapt using reinforcement learning. The tube shape itself would evolve procedurally, creating novel geometric challenges.

Classification

Series: SCR — Scrolling Shooter

While Tempest doesn't scroll in the traditional sense, its tube-rim perspective and shoot-inward mechanic place it in the abstract shooter lineage. The "descent" between levels is a form of forward scrolling.

Core Mechanic

Tube-rim movement with inward shooting. The player moves in 1D (around the rim) while enemies approach from a vanishing point. The geometric tube shape defines the tactical space. Lane management — knowing which lanes are dangerous — is the core skill.

What This Prototype Tests

  • Tube perspective — does the pseudo-3D depth illusion work with vector-style lines?
  • Lane-based combat — is lane switching fast and responsive enough?
  • Enemy variety — do Flippers, Tankers, and Spikers create distinct threats?
  • Descent mechanic — does the level-end transition feel rewarding?

Prototype Notes

This is a sketch rated 4/5. Vector-style neon graphics on black background. Five tube shapes: circle (16 segments), square (16), triangle (12), cross (20), flat (16). Three enemy types: Flippers, Tankers, Spikers. Descent animation between levels with spike avoidance. Progressive difficulty with more enemies and faster climbing per level.
