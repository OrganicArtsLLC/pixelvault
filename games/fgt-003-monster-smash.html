FGT-003 · MONSTER SMASH

FGT — Fighter / Combat Prototype #003 ★★★★☆

How to Play

You are George, a giant ape terrorizing the city. Destroy all buildings to advance to the next city. Eat people to restore health. Avoid military attacks.

  • ← → — Walk left / right
  • ↑ — Climb building (when adjacent) / jump
  • ↓ — Climb down / drop from building
  • Z — Punch (damages building section in front of you)
  • X — Grab & eat person in window (restores health)
  • R — Restart game
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Climb to eat: People appear in windows — climb up and press X when next to one for health.
  • Punch strategically: Destroy lower floors first to collapse upper sections for bonus points.
  • Watch for military: Helicopters and tanks deal significant damage. Punch helicopters when close.
  • Don’t fall: If you’re climbing when a floor collapses beneath you, you’ll take fall damage.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Rampage (1986, Brian Colin, Bally Midway), King of the Monsters (1991, SNK)

Rampage was created by Brian Colin and Jeff Nauman at Bally Midway in 1986. Inspired by classic monster movies — King Kong, Godzilla, The Wolfman — it let up to three players simultaneously control giant monsters destroying a city. The game inverted the standard arcade formula: instead of defending against monsters, you WERE the monster.

The three characters — George (ape/King Kong), Lizzie (lizard/Godzilla), and Ralph (werewolf) — were humans mutated by various experiments gone wrong. When a monster’s health reached zero, it reverted to a naked human covering itself, shuffling off screen. This dark humor was central to the game’s appeal.

The building destruction mechanic was groundbreaking. Each building was a grid of destructible sections, each with independent hit points. Players could strategically demolish structures floor by floor, and buildings would visually crumble as sections were destroyed. This tile-based destruction model influenced countless games that followed.

King of the Monsters (1991, SNK) took the kaiju fantasy in a different direction — giant monster versus giant monster wrestling matches — but both games shared the core power fantasy of urban destruction.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (1980–1997)

Contemporary AI: In 1986, expert systems were booming and connectionism was being revived by Rumelhart and McClelland’s PDP volumes. But no AI could handle the kind of spatial reasoning and destruction simulation that Rampage presented to players.

Hidden Intelligence: Rampage’s building destruction uses a tile-based damage model where each section can be independently destroyed, creating emergent collapse patterns. When lower floors are destroyed, upper floors become unsupported and collapse — an early physics simulation. This granular destruction prefigured voxel-based damage systems (Red Faction, 2001) and physics-driven demolition (Battlefield: Bad Company, 2008). The military AI escalates based on player threat level, creating a dynamic difficulty curve driven by the player’s own destructive success.

Modern Lens: Training an RL agent on Rampage would require understanding structural integrity, resource management (health vs. risk), multi-objective optimization (destroy buildings vs. avoid damage vs. eat civilians), and spatial reasoning about climbing positions. The game’s destruction model is essentially a simplified finite element analysis.

Classification

Series: FGT — Fighter / Combat. Rampage is classified as a fighter because the core mechanic is direct physical combat with the environment and military enemies, not maze navigation or puzzle solving. The monster’s punching, climbing, and eating actions are all combat verbs.

Related prototypes: FGT-001 (Joust) shares the height-advantage combat mechanic. SHT-002 (Berzerk) shares the military-vs-player dynamic but from opposite perspective.

Prototype Notes

This prototype focuses on the core destruction loop: punch buildings, eat people, dodge military. Buildings use a tile grid with per-section hit points and collapse physics. The military escalation is simplified to periodic spawns with increasing frequency. Single monster (George) for clarity.

Intentional simplifications: no 3-player mode, no between-level map, no bonus rounds. The prototype isolates the essential risk-reward loop of climbing (expose yourself to military fire to reach food) versus ground-level punching (safer but no healing).
