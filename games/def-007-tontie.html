DEF-007 · TONTIE

DEF — Deflection Prototype #007 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Enemies pop up on a 3×3 grid mapped to your numpad. Press the matching key (1–9) to hammer them before they escape.

  • 789 — Top row (left, center, right)
  • 456 — Middle row
  • 123 — Bottom row
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Enemy Types

  • Green (normal): 1 hit to defeat. +100 points.
  • Blue (armored): 2 hits to defeat. +250 points.
  • Red (bomb): Do NOT hit! Hitting a bomb costs a life. Let it disappear.
  • Gold (bonus): 1 hit, +500 points. Appears rarely and disappears fast.

Tips

  • Build combos: Consecutive successful hits multiply your score. Missing or hitting a bomb resets the combo.
  • Learn the numpad: Your fingers will develop spatial memory — trust the muscle memory.
  • Watch for bombs: They look red and angry. Resist the urge to hit everything.
  • Prioritize gold enemies: They vanish quickly but are worth 5× a normal hit.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Whac-A-Mole (1975, Aaron Fechter) → Tontie (2004, On Nakayama / Eyezmaze) → keyboard rhythm games

On Nakayama, the Japanese Flash game auteur behind the beloved GROW series, created Tontie in 2004. While most Flash games used mouse input, Tontie mapped its 3×3 grid to the numpad — a brilliant design choice that created a tactile, physical relationship between the player and the game. Your right hand became a hammer, each finger assigned to a position.

Eyezmaze (eye-maze) was one of the most distinctive Flash game sites of the 2000s, known for elegant puzzle games with hand-drawn art. Tontie stood out as an action game in a catalogue of cerebral puzzles, proving Nakayama's range as a designer.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 4 — Statistical Learning (1993–2006)

Contemporary AI: SVMs, Random Forests, early deep learning experiments. Game AI increasingly uses learned models rather than hand-coded rules.

Hidden Intelligence: Tontie is a stimulus-response compatibility experiment. The numpad's spatial layout (7-8-9 top, 1-2-3 bottom) mirrors the game grid, creating a natural mapping. As speed increases, the player must bypass conscious spatial reasoning and rely on learned motor patterns — the same automaticity that drives typing speed. The game trains a neural mapping from visual position to finger activation.

The Convergence: Modern AI uses spatial attention mechanisms (transformers, 2017) that learn where to "look" in input space. Tontie trains the human equivalent: selective spatial attention mapped to motor output. The numpad becomes a 9-key attention selector.

If AI Had Designed This: Adaptive spawn patterns based on the player's per-cell reaction times — spawning more enemies where the player is slowest, creating personalized training. This is essentially spaced repetition applied to motor learning.

Classification

Series: DEF — Deflection

Tontie extends the Whac-A-Mole lineage from mouse-click targeting to keyboard-spatial targeting. The "deflection" is conceptual — you intercept enemies before they escape.

Core Mechanic

Numpad whacking on a 3×3 grid. Enemies appear, you press the matching key. Multiple enemy types add decision-making to the reaction task. Speed escalation creates progressive difficulty.

What This Prototype Tests

  • Numpad-to-grid spatial mapping — does it feel natural?
  • Enemy type differentiation — can you distinguish friend from foe at speed?
  • Combo system — does it reward skilled play without punishing learning?
  • Speed curve — does difficulty ramp feel fair?
