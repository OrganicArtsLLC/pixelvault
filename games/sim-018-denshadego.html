How to Play

Drive a Japanese commuter train along five stations. Control your speed and brake precisely to stop at each platform marker.

  • ↑ — Increase throttle (5 notches)
  • ↓ — Increase brake (8 notches, plus emergency)
  • Space — Release throttle/brake to neutral
  • R — Restart route
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Scoring

  • Perfect stop (within 0.5m) — 100 points
  • Good stop (within 1m) — 50 points
  • Acceptable (within 3m) — 10 points
  • Overrun / underrun (beyond 3m) — penalty
  • On-time arrival — 50 bonus points
  • Speed limit violation — 5 point penalty per second

History

Densha de Go! was released by Taito in 1996 as an arcade game with a dedicated train controller peripheral — a combined throttle and brake handle that mimicked real Japanese train controls. The game became a cultural phenomenon in Japan, spawning over 20 sequels across arcade, PlayStation, Nintendo DS, and mobile platforms.

The series captures something deeply Japanese: the pride of precision. Japanese rail operators are world-famous for punctuality — the average Shinkansen delay is measured in seconds. Densha de Go! turned this professional pride into gameplay, where the satisfaction comes not from speed or combat but from exact timing, smooth braking, and the quiet dignity of a perfect platform stop.

Real Japanese train drivers train for years to master the art of stopping within centimeters of the platform marker. The game distills this into an accessible experience where anyone can feel what it's like to shoulder that responsibility.

AI Archaeology

From an AI perspective, train driving is a continuous control problem with hard constraints. The driver must solve a trajectory optimization: given current speed, distance to stop, track gradient, and weather conditions, compute the optimal braking curve. Professional drivers internalize this as intuition; control systems formalize it as PID loops and model predictive control. The game sits at the intersection of human skill and algorithmic precision — the same space where autonomous driving research lives today.

Prototype Notes

Simplified to five stations with speed limit zones. Physics model uses basic acceleration/deceleration with brake notch curves. Scenery is procedurally generated parallax layers. The feeling of precision stopping is the core loop — everything else serves that moment of arrival.
