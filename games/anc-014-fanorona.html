ANC-014 · FANORONA

ANC — Ancient Games Prototype #014 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Play Fanorona against an AI opponent. You are White (bottom). Capture all of your opponent's pieces to win.

  • Select: Click one of your pieces to see valid moves highlighted.
  • Move: Click a highlighted intersection to move your piece there.
  • Approach capture: Move toward an enemy line — all enemies in that direction are captured.
  • Withdrawal capture: Move away from an enemy line — all enemies in the opposite direction are captured.
  • Choice: When both approach and withdrawal are available, click the capture direction indicator.
  • Chain captures: After a capture, if another capture is available with the same piece, you must continue.
  • Non-capturing moves: Only allowed when no captures are available anywhere.
  • R — Restart game
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Alquerque (~1000 BCE, Middle East) → Fanorona (~1680, Madagascar)

Fanorona is the national board game of Madagascar, believed to have evolved from Alquerque, which was brought to the island by Arab traders. The game became deeply embedded in Malagasy culture — legend holds that the fate of the Merina kingdom was once decided by a game of Fanorona rather than battle.

The Board: The distinctive 9×5 board with its mix of orthogonal and diagonal connections (called "strong" and "weak" intersections) creates asymmetric geometry that fundamentally shapes strategy. Diagonal connections appear in a checkerboard pattern, making some positions more connected than others.

Cultural Significance: Fanorona was traditionally played with pebbles and seeds on boards scratched into stone. Several ancient game boards carved into rock can still be found across Madagascar. The game was associated with divination and royal decision-making.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 2 — Traditional Malagasy (~1680)

Unique Decision Space: Fanorona's dual-direction capture is computationally fascinating. Unlike checkers or chess where a capture's outcome is determined by the move itself, in Fanorona the same move can produce two entirely different board states depending on whether you capture by approach or withdrawal. This effectively doubles the branching factor at capture moves.

Forced Captures: The mandatory capture rule, combined with chain captures, means that a single turn can dramatically transform the board. A well-planned chain can capture 10+ pieces in one turn, creating explosive tactical swings that reward forward planning.

Solved Status: Fanorona was weakly solved in 2008 by Maarten Schadd et al. using proof-number search. The game is a draw with perfect play from both sides, but the proof required enormous computational resources despite the relatively small board.

Classification

Series: ANC — Ancient Games

Games with roots stretching back centuries or millennia. Fanorona represents the rich board game tradition of Madagascar and the broader Indian Ocean world.

Core Mechanic

Dual-direction capture (approach/withdrawal), mandatory captures, chain captures on a partially-connected grid. The approach/withdrawal choice creates a unique decision layer absent from other capture games.

Prototype Notes

This is a sketch rated 4/5. Standard Fanorona rules on the 9×5 board with diagonal connections on alternating intersections. AI uses minimax with alpha-beta pruning. Captures are animated with brief highlights showing which pieces are removed. The approach/withdrawal choice is presented as clickable direction indicators when both options are available.
