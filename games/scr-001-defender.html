SCR — Scroll Shooter Prototype #001 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Protect 10 humans on the ground from alien abduction. Destroy all aliens to clear the sector. If all humans die, surviving aliens mutate into deadly swarmers.

  • ← → — Fly left/right (momentum-based)
  • ↑ ↓ — Change altitude
  • Space — Shoot (rapid fire laser)
  • Z — Smart bomb (destroys all on-screen enemies, limited supply)
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Minimap: The scanner bar at the bottom shows the entire 3200px world. Your position is the bright dot.
  • Abduction: Landers grab humans and carry them up. Shoot the lander to save the human (they’ll fall back down).
  • Mutants: If a lander reaches the top with a human, it becomes a fast-moving mutant.
  • Smart bombs: Save them for emergencies when swarmed.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Defender (1981) → Gradius (1985) → R-Type (1987)

Defender (1981) by Eugene Jarvis at Williams Electronics was considered too complex for arcades. It had 5 buttons and a joystick. Players hated it at first. Then they mastered it and it became one of the highest-grossing arcade games ever.

Two massive innovations: (1) The game world was larger than the screen, requiring free scrolling — a first in arcade games. (2) The minimap (scanner) was invented specifically for this game to give players awareness of threats beyond the viewport.

The civilian protection mechanic added moral weight: losing humans changes the strategic equation. This “protect someone other than yourself” pattern appears in escort missions, base defense, and tower defense games to this day.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (1978–1983)

Contemporary AI: Limited to discrete, fully observable environments. No concept of partial observability in practical systems. The POMDP framework existed theoretically (1965) but wasn't practically solved.

Hidden Intelligence: Defender invented the world-larger-than-screen problem — partial observability at arcade speed. The minimap is a dimensionality reduction technique: compressing the full world state into a small, interpretable summary. This is exactly what autoencoders (1986) and attention mechanisms (2017) do in AI. Eugene Jarvis solved it with pure design intuition.

The Convergence: POMDPs (Partially Observable Markov Decision Processes) were formalized in 1965 but not practically solved until the 1990s. Defender built a playable POMDP in 1981 and solved the information-display problem through game design before AI had the mathematical tools.

If AI Had Designed This: Optimized the minimap information density — empirically testing which alert modalities best maintain player situation awareness at maximum speed.

Classification

Series: SCR — Scroll Shooter

Games where the play area scrolls (horizontally or vertically) through a world larger than the screen. The player navigates continuous space while engaging enemies. The tension: manage what you can see and anticipate what you can’t.

Core Mechanic

Free-scrolling combat with civilian protection. 3200px world with bidirectional scrolling. Aliens attempt to abduct ground-based humans. Player must prioritize: fight aliens or save civilians. Smart bombs as panic button (limited, powerful).

What This Prototype Tests

  • Free-scroll feel (does the world feel larger than the screen?)
  • Minimap effectiveness (can you locate threats before they’re on screen?)
  • Abduction drama (does rescuing humans feel urgent and rewarding?)
  • Alien variety (lander, swarm, mutant — do they feel different?)
