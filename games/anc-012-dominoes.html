ANC-012 · DOMINOES

ANC — Ancient Games Prototype #012 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Play dominoes against an AI opponent. Match pip values to extend the chain. First to empty your hand wins.

  • Select: Click a tile in your hand to select it (highlighted in yellow).
  • Play: Click the left or right end of the chain to place your selected tile.
  • Draw: Press D or click the boneyard to draw a tile when you cannot play.
  • Doubles: The highest double starts the game. Doubles are placed crosswise.
  • R — New game
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Chinese Dominoes (~1200 CE) → European Dominoes (~1700s, Italy) → Block Game → Draw Game → Modern Variants

Dominoes originated in China around the 12th century, possibly derived from Chinese dice. The game arrived in Europe through Italy in the 18th century, where the familiar 28-tile double-six set became standard. The name likely comes from the French word for a black-and-white hooded cloak worn by Christian priests.

The Draw Game: The variant implemented here adds strategic depth to the basic Block Game by allowing players to draw from the boneyard (undealt tiles) when they cannot play. This creates hidden information — you never know exactly what your opponent holds.

Cultural Impact: Dominoes became one of the most popular games worldwide, especially in Latin America, the Caribbean, and Southeast Asia. In many cultures, dominoes is as much a social ritual as a game — played in parks, cafes, and family gatherings with passionate intensity.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 2 — Expert Systems (rule-based play with probabilistic inference)

Hidden Information: Unlike chess or checkers, dominoes involves imperfect information. You can see your own tiles and the played chain, but your opponent's hand and the boneyard contents are hidden. Expert play requires Bayesian reasoning — tracking which tiles have been played, inferring what your opponent might hold based on their draws and passes.

Counting Tiles: Strong domino players mentally track all 28 tiles. When an opponent draws or passes, it reveals information. If they pass when a 5 is open, they have no 5s. This constraint propagation is the same technique used in expert systems and constraint satisfaction problems.

Endgame Planning: Late-game dominoes becomes a pure logic puzzle — with enough tiles revealed, you can sometimes prove whether a winning line exists. This transition from probabilistic to deterministic reasoning mirrors how AI systems shift strategies as information accumulates.
