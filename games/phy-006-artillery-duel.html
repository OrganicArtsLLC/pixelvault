PHY-006 · ARTILLERY DUEL

PHY — Physics Prototype #006 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Take turns firing artillery at the enemy team. Destroy terrain and eliminate all enemy worms to win!

  • ← / → — Move worm
  • ↑ / ↓ — Aim angle
  • Space (hold) — Charge power meter
  • Enter — Fire weapon
  • 1 — Bazooka (arced projectile)
  • 2 — Grenade (bounces, timed fuse)
  • 3 — Shotgun (short range spread)
  • R — Restart game
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Wind: Check the wind indicator at the top — it affects projectile flight.
  • Terrain: Explosions carve craters. Use this to drop enemies or create cover.
  • Grenades: They bounce and explode after 3 seconds — great for tricky angles.
  • Shotgun: Devastating at close range, useless at distance.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Gorillas (1991, Microsoft QBasic) → Scorched Earth (1991) → Worms (1995, Team17)

The artillery game is one of the oldest computer game genres, dating to early mainframe games. Scorched Earth (1991) refined the concept with destructible terrain, multiple weapons, and wind effects. Worms (1995) by Team17 added character, humor, and team-based play, becoming a beloved multiplayer franchise.

The core loop is ancient but deep: angle, power, fire. Wind adds a variable that prevents memorization. Destructible terrain means the game state is never the same twice, creating emergent tactical situations.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 4 — Statistical Learning (1993–2006)

Contemporary AI: Statistical machine learning, SVMs, early reinforcement learning. TD-Gammon demonstrated RL in complex games.

Hidden Intelligence: Worms’ AI must solve an inverse kinematics problem: given a target position, wind, and terrain, compute the angle and power to hit it. This is a simplified ballistic trajectory optimization — the same class of problem that guidance systems solve. The AI must also reason about terrain destruction as a secondary objective.

The Convergence: Non-stationary environments are a fundamental challenge in reinforcement learning. In Worms, every explosion permanently changes the state space. Modern RL research on sim-to-real transfer faces the same issue: the training environment never perfectly matches deployment. Worms was an early, intuitive demonstration of this principle.

Classification

Genre: Artillery / Turn-Based Strategy

Input Complexity: Medium — angle, power, weapon selection, movement

State Complexity: High — destructible terrain creates unique game states

Prototype Notes

Pixel-based terrain destruction using ImageData. Projectile physics with gravity and wind. Simple AI uses trajectory calculation with randomized error. Worms fall with gravity after terrain changes. Three weapon types with distinct tactical roles.
