ANC-020 · JANGGI (Korean Chess)

ANC — Ancient Games Prototype #020 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Play Janggi against an AI opponent. You are Blue/Green (bottom). Click a piece to select it, then click a highlighted intersection to move.

  • General (將/帥): Moves one step orthogonally or diagonally within the 3×3 palace.
  • Guard (士): Moves one step orthogonally or diagonally within the palace.
  • Chariot (車): Moves like a rook — any distance orthogonally. Can also move along palace diagonals.
  • Horse (馬): Moves one step orthogonal, then one diagonal outward. Blocked if the orthogonal step is occupied.
  • Elephant (象): Moves one step orthogonal, then two steps diagonal outward. Blocked if any intermediate step is occupied.
  • Cannon (包): Must jump over exactly one piece (the screen) to move or capture. Cannot jump over another cannon. Cannot capture another cannon.
  • Soldier (卒/兵): Moves one step forward or sideways. Never backward.
  • R — Restart game

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Chaturanga (~600 CE, India) → Xiangqi (China) → Janggi (~900 CE, Korea)

Janggi evolved from Chinese chess (Xiangqi) but developed its own distinct character. The most dramatic difference is the cannon: in Xiangqi, cannons only need to jump to capture; in Janggi, cannons must jump to move at all. This single rule change creates an entirely different tactical landscape.

Other key differences from Xiangqi: there is no river restriction on pieces, elephants have a larger range, and generals can face each other across the board (the "flying general" rule does not apply). The game was historically played by Korean nobility and military strategists.

Janggi remains hugely popular in Korea today, with professional players, televised tournaments, and a rich body of opening theory. It is considered one of the deepest strategy games in East Asian tradition.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 2 — Traditional game (~900 CE)

Janggi presents unique challenges for AI compared to Western chess. The cannon's context-dependent movement — its legal moves change based on the positions of ALL other pieces — makes static evaluation far more complex. A cannon that dominates the board in the midgame may become useless in the endgame as jump screens disappear.

The branching factor in Janggi is comparable to chess (~30-40 moves per position), but the evaluation function must account for the highly non-linear value of cannons. Early computer Janggi programs struggled precisely because material-based evaluation fails when a piece's value depends on board configuration.

Classification

Series: ANC — Ancient Games

Games with roots stretching back centuries or millennia. Janggi represents the Korean branch of the chess family tree, with innovations that make it a fundamentally different tactical experience.

Core Mechanic

Cannon jumping — a piece that must vault over exactly one other piece to move or capture. This creates dynamic, configuration-dependent tactics unlike any other chess variant.
