How to Play

Kabaddi is a contact sport where a lone raider enters enemy territory, tags opponents, and must return before their breath runs out.

  • ↑ ↓ ← → — Move your player
  • Space — Tackle attempt (when defending)
  • R — Restart match
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Raiding: Cross the center line into enemy territory. Tag opponents by touching them. Return to your half before the 10-second breath timer expires. Each tag scores 1 point. If the timer runs out or defenders surround you, you are out and the opponent scores.

Defending: You control one defender (highlighted). Move to intercept the AI raider. Press Space near the raider to attempt a tackle. A successful tackle scores 1 point for your team.

History

Kabaddi is one of the oldest sports in the world, with roots in the Indian subcontinent dating back roughly 4,000 years. The name derives from the Tamil word "kai-pidi" meaning "holding hands." Ancient forms were used as training for self-defense and group hunting.

The modern codified game emerged in the 1920s. It became a demonstration sport at the 1936 Berlin Olympics and was included in the Asian Games from 1990. The Pro Kabaddi League, launched in India in 2014, transformed it into a televised spectacle rivalling cricket in viewership.

The sport's genius is its asymmetry: one raider versus many defenders, constrained by the "cant" — the raider must chant "kabaddi, kabaddi" in one breath, creating a natural time limit. This single rule generates extraordinary tactical depth.

AI Archaeology

Era 3 — Expert Systems

Kabaddi's raid mechanic is a natural pursuit-evasion problem. Defenders must coordinate zone coverage while the raider optimizes a path that maximizes tags under a strict time constraint — a variant of the travelling salesman problem with adversarial obstacles and a hard deadline.

The raider's decision — how deep to go, how many to tag, when to retreat — maps to risk-reward optimization under uncertainty. Early expert systems could model this with rule-based state machines: "if raider is within X distance, converge; if near boundary, spread."

Prototype Notes

  • Top-down court view with center line dividing two halves
  • 6v6 teams; alternate between raiding and defending each turn
  • Breath timer (10 seconds) creates the core tension of every raid
  • Tagged players flash and leave the court; they return next round
  • AI defenders use zone-based convergence when raider enters territory
  • AI raider uses opportunistic tag-and-retreat with risk awareness
