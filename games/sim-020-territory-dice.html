SIM-020 · TERRITORY DICE

SIM — Simulation Prototype #020 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Conquer all territories on the hex map. You are Blue — defeat Red, Green, and Yellow AI opponents.

  • Click one of your territories (must have 2+ dice) to select it
  • Click an adjacent enemy territory to attack
  • Both sides roll all their dice — higher total wins and captures the territory
  • Click the End Turn button (or press E) when done attacking
  • Bonus dice (equal to your largest connected territory group) are distributed at end of turn
  • R — Restart with a new random map
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Strategy Tips

  • Keep your territories connected — your bonus dice depend on largest connected group size.
  • Attack when you have more dice than the defender for better odds.
  • 8 dice is the maximum per territory — excess bonus dice are lost. Spread out!
  • Sometimes it is better to hold back than to attack and leave a territory with 1 die.
  • Watch the scoreboard — eliminate weak players before they recover.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Risk (1957, Albert Lamorisse) → Dicewars (2005, Taro Ito, Flash game, Japan)

Dicewars, created by Taro Ito in 2005 as a Flash browser game, brilliantly distilled Risk into a fast-playing strategy game. By replacing armies with stacked dice and automating combat resolution, Ito eliminated the tedious bookkeeping of Risk while preserving its core territorial tension.

The game became a viral hit on Flash game portals, spawning numerous clones and inspiring the "dice stacking" mechanic in later strategy games. Its design philosophy — maximum strategic depth from minimal rules — exemplifies Japanese game design sensibility.

Risk itself descends from the French board game La Conquete du Monde (1957) by Albert Lamorisse, which introduced territorial conquest via dice combat to the mass market.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 5 — Deep Learning (2000-2010)

The AI challenge in Dicewars is probabilistic reasoning combined with graph analysis. Each attack has a calculable win probability based on the sum distributions of N vs M six-sided dice. Optimal play requires evaluating multi-step attack sequences where each battle outcome affects subsequent possibilities.

The bonus dice mechanic (largest connected group) transforms this from pure probability into a graph connectivity problem. The AI must maintain and expand connected territory components, sometimes sacrificing advantageous attacks to preserve connectivity.

This intersection of probability, graph theory, and multi-agent dynamics makes Dicewars a surprisingly rich domain for AI research despite its simple rules.

Prototype Notes

This prototype generates random hex-grid maps with ~30 territories divided among 4 players. Each territory displays its dice count visually as stacked cubes. The AI evaluates attacks based on win probability (favoring 70%+ odds), connectivity preservation, and opportunistic elimination of weakened opponents. Random map generation ensures high replayability — no two games play alike.
