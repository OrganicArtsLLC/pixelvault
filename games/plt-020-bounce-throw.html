PLT — Platformer Prototype #020 ★★★★☆

How to Play

You are Yumetaro, a small green creature. Throw your bouncing star to defeat enemies, activate switches, and — most importantly — ride it across gaps and up to high platforms.

  • ← → — Move left/right
  • Space — Jump
  • Z — Throw star (aim with direction keys)
  • R — Restart level
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

The Star Mechanic

  • Bouncing physics: The star bounces off walls, floors, and ceilings following reflection physics (angle in = angle out).
  • Ride the star: Jump onto the bouncing star to ride it like a moving platform. This is the key to crossing gaps and reaching high places.
  • Destroy enemies: The star destroys any enemy it hits while bouncing.
  • Slopes redirect: Slopes change the star's trajectory — use them to aim your bounces precisely.
  • Think ahead: The real puzzle is predicting where the star will bounce. Throw it so it ends up where you need a platform.

Tips

  • You can only have one star active at a time. Wait for it to expire or collect it back.
  • The star lasts about 6 seconds before fading.
  • On slopes, the star follows the surface angle — use this to redirect bounces.
  • Some gaps can only be crossed by riding the star. Throw it, then jump on!

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Gimmick! (1992, Sunsoft) — Famicom, Japan & Scandinavia only

Gimmick! (known as Mr. Gimmick in Scandinavia) was developed by Sunsoft and released for the Famicom in 1992, with a limited PAL release in Scandinavia. It never received a North American release, making it one of the rarest and most sought-after NES-era platformers.

The game is widely regarded as one of the greatest platformers of the 8-bit era. Its star-throwing mechanic was revolutionary — a real-time physics simulation running on 8-bit hardware, where the star bounced off surfaces following proper reflection angles. Players could ride the bouncing star, creating emergent puzzle-platforming that was years ahead of its time.

Sunsoft's programming team achieved remarkable technical feats: smooth parallax scrolling, a sophisticated physics engine, and one of the best soundtracks on the Famicom (composed by Naoki Kodaka). The game's obscurity is a tragedy of distribution rather than quality — it represents the absolute peak of what the Famicom hardware could achieve.

Each throw demands mental modeling of reflection angles across multiple surfaces — the same geometric reasoning that underlies early computational physics and trajectory optimization in ML. The emergent puzzle-platforming (throw here, bounce off that slope, ride across the gap) mirrors how reinforcement learning agents must plan multi-step physical interactions to achieve goals.
