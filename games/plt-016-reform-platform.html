PLT-016 · REFORM PLATFORM

PLT — Platformer Prototype #016 ★★★★☆

How to Play

You are a frog leaping between lily pads on a pond. Every pad you land on begins to shrink and will eventually sink beneath the water. Collect flowers and stars for points, but never stay still — the ground is always disappearing beneath you.

  • ← → — Move left/right
  • ↑ / Space — Jump
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Keep moving: Pads shrink faster the longer you stand on them. Jump early and often.
  • New pads grow: Fresh lily pads periodically appear — watch for bright green pads emerging from the water.
  • Collect items: Flowers (⚘) and stars (★) appear on pads. Grab them before the pad sinks.
  • Water = death: Fall in the water and you lose a life. You have 3 lives.
  • Levels: Each level adds more pads but they shrink faster. Survive the restructuring.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Perestroika (1990, Nikita Skripkin / Locis Software, USSR) ← Frogger (1981, Konami)

Perestroika (Russian: Перестро́йка, “restructuring”) was created by Nikita Skripkin at Locis Software in 1990, during the final years of the Soviet Union. The game was an unmistakable political allegory: a frog (the citizen) leaping between lily pads (institutions of Soviet society) that shrink and sink beneath you. The message was clear — the platforms of the old order were crumbling, and survival meant constant adaptation.

Originally written for MS-DOS, it became one of the most famous Soviet-era computer games. The game spread through floppy-disk copying networks across the USSR and Eastern Bloc. It captured the anxiety and dark humor of an era when the entire social contract was dissolving in real time. The title directly referenced Gorbachev’s reform program that was reshaping (and ultimately dismantling) the Soviet system.

The mechanical lineage connects to Frogger (1981) — both feature a small creature navigating a hostile environment by jumping between safe zones. But where Frogger’s traffic is cyclical and predictable, Perestroika’s platforms decay irreversibly, creating a one-way ratchet toward chaos that mirrors historical entropy.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Machine Learning (1980–2000)

Contemporary AI: Expert systems, early neural networks, connectionism vs. symbolicism debate.

Insight: The shrinking-platform mechanic creates a non-stationary environment — a concept central to reinforcement learning research. An agent trained on early game states would find its policy invalidated as platforms decay, requiring continuous adaptation. This mirrors the challenge of concept drift in machine learning, where the underlying data distribution shifts over time. The game is essentially a physical metaphor for training in non-stationary environments.

Prototype Notes

This prototype implements the core shrinking-platform loop with a frog character, collectible items, progressive levels, and the signature mechanic of pads that decay after landing. New pads grow periodically to prevent total platform extinction. The political metaphor is preserved through the escalating instability of each level.
