How to Play

Defeat the AI opponent in best-of-3 rounds combat. Use punches, kicks, special moves, and combos to deplete the enemy's health bar. Block to reduce damage. Win 2 rounds to claim victory.

  • A D — Move left / right
  • W — Jump
  • S — Crouch
  • J — Punch
  • K — Kick
  • L — Block (hold)
  • ↓→ + J — Projectile (fireball)
  • ↓→ + K — Uppercut
  • ↓← + K — Sweep
  • R — Restart

Tips

  • Combos: Chain hits quickly for bonus damage. The combo counter shows consecutive hits.
  • Blocking: Reduces damage significantly but you cannot move or attack while blocking.
  • Spacing: Use projectiles at range, punches and kicks up close.
  • Uppercut: Powerful move that launches the opponent — great combo finisher.
  • Finish Him: When your opponent's health is critical, land the final blow.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Street Fighter II (1991, Capcom) → Mortal Kombat (1992, Midway) → Mortal Kombat II (1993)

Mortal Kombat (1992) by Ed Boon and John Tobias was Midway's answer to Capcom's Street Fighter II. It distinguished itself with digitized actor sprites (giving it a photorealistic look for the era), a darker tone, and its infamous "Fatality" finishing moves.

The game used a simplified special move system compared to Street Fighter II's quarter-circle motions, making specials more accessible. Its AI was notoriously input-reading — at higher difficulties the CPU would react to your button presses before the animation even played.

MK's cultural impact was enormous: it directly led to the creation of the ESRB rating system in 1994, fundamentally changing how games were marketed and sold.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (1980–1993)

Contemporary AI: Expert systems peak. Neural networks beginning resurgence (backpropagation rediscovered 1986).

Hidden Intelligence: Fighting game AI presents a unique design challenge: it must lose convincingly. Pure optimization would create unbeatable opponents (the CPU can read inputs instantly). Instead, MK's AI uses deliberate delay windows — it "sees" your input, waits N frames, then reacts. At low difficulty, N is large (slow reactions). At high difficulty, N approaches zero (instant reads). This creates an illusion of intelligence while actually being a carefully tuned handicap system.

The Convergence: Modern fighting game AI research uses reinforcement learning to train opponents that are challenging but beatable — essentially learning the same "calibrated imperfection" that MK's designers hand-tuned. The goal isn't to win; it's to create the feeling of a fair fight.

If AI Had Designed This: Adaptive difficulty that models the player's skill in real-time, adjusting reaction windows dynamically to keep win rates near 50% — a technique now used in modern matchmaking (Elo, TrueSkill).
