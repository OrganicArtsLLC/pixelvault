SRV-004 · DELIVERY RUN

SRV — Service / Dispatch Prototype #004 ★★★★☆

How to Play

You're a paperboy on a bicycle. Deliver newspapers to subscriber houses (blue) while dodging obstacles on the street. Miss a subscriber and they cancel. Survive 7 days to complete your route.

  • ↑ ↓ — Change lane (sidewalk, road, far sidewalk)
  • ← → — Slow down / speed up
  • Space — Throw newspaper
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Aim for mailboxes: Throw from the middle lane for the best arc to subscriber mailboxes.
  • Conserve papers: You only get a few extras each day — don't waste them.
  • Watch subscriptions: Every missed subscriber cancels — fewer targets means fewer points tomorrow.
  • Speed control: Slow down near clusters of houses, speed up through empty stretches.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Paperboy (1985, Atari Games)

Paperboy (1985) was designed by John Salwitz and Dave Ralston at Atari Games. It used a unique handlebar controller and an isometric perspective that was technically ambitious for its era. The game was one of the first to simulate a mundane real-world job — newspaper delivery — and turn it into compelling gameplay.

The subscription mechanic was revolutionary: your performance directly affected tomorrow's difficulty. Miss a house, lose a subscriber, have fewer targets and less reason to take risks. It created a natural difficulty curve driven by the player's own performance — a feedback loop that modern roguelikes would rediscover decades later.

The game's suburban setting was deliberately ordinary. No aliens, no dungeons — just a kid on a bike, dodging dogs and cars, trying to hit mailboxes. The mundanity was the point.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (1980–1997)

Contemporary AI: In 1985, expert systems were at their peak. MYCIN diagnosed diseases, R1/XCON configured computers. But no AI system could handle the multi-objective real-time optimization that Paperboy demands — simultaneously tracking delivery targets, obstacle avoidance, resource management, and route planning.

Hidden Intelligence: Paperboy is a multi-objective optimization problem disguised as a newspaper delivery game. The player must: (1) maximize deliveries (accuracy), (2) minimize collisions (survival), (3) manage limited resources (paper count), and (4) maintain the subscriber base (meta-game). These four objectives conflict — taking risks to deliver increases collision probability.

The Feedback Loop: The subscription mechanic creates what control theory calls a "closed-loop system" — output (delivery performance) feeds back into input (subscriber count), which affects future output (available targets). Poor performance compounds; excellence stabilizes. This is the same dynamic that governs customer retention in SaaS businesses.

If AI Had Designed This: A 1985 expert system could have added route preview — showing upcoming houses and obstacles — giving the player planning horizon. The original game's charm was in its immediacy: react, throw, dodge, repeat.

Classification

Series: SRV — Service / Dispatch

Games where the player manages delivery or dispatch across multiple simultaneous channels. The core tension: serve the right targets while surviving the environment.

Core Mechanic

Side-scrolling delivery with resource management. Auto-scrolling street, 3 lanes, throw papers at subscriber mailboxes, dodge obstacles. Subscription loss creates a performance feedback loop.
