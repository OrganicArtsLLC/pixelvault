PUZ — Puzzle Prototype #002 ★★★★☆

How to Play

A column of 3 colored jewels falls. Position it and cycle the jewel order to create horizontal, vertical, or diagonal lines of 3+ matching colors.

  • ← → or A D — Move column left/right
  • ↑ or W — Cycle jewel order (rotate colors within column)
  • ↓ or S — Soft drop (faster fall)
  • Space — Hard drop (instant)
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Diagonals count: Unlike most match-3 games, diagonal lines clear too.
  • Chains: Gravity pulls jewels down after a match, potentially creating chain reactions.
  • Cycle wisely: The cycle verb is your only tool for color placement — master it.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Columns (1990, Jay Geertsen/Sega) → Puyo Puyo (1991) → Bejeweled (2001)

Columns was Sega's answer to Tetris, bundled with the Game Gear. It shifted the puzzle paradigm from spatial packing to color alignment. The single verb — cycle jewel order — was inspired by slot machines: simple input, complex positional consequences.

Columns fathered the match-3 genre, which became the most commercially successful game mechanic in mobile gaming history (Candy Crush, Bejeweled, Puzzle & Dragons).

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 4 — Statistical Learning (1990–1997)

Contemporary AI: Pattern recognition advancing. Decision trees, early neural networks. AI could classify but struggled with real-time combinatorial planning.

Hidden Intelligence: Columns is a constraint satisfaction problem — place colors to satisfy alignment constraints across three axes (horizontal, vertical, diagonal). Unlike Tetris's spatial packing, this is a graph coloring variant. The match-3 mechanic became a $10B mobile game category, proving that color matching is a universal cognitive primitive humans find deeply satisfying.

The Convergence: Modern match-3 games use ML for difficulty tuning and player retention modeling. The mechanic that started as a simple puzzle now drives some of the most sophisticated player behavior prediction systems in gaming.

If AI Had Designed This: Adaptive color distribution that monitors the board state and adjusts incoming piece colors to maintain optimal tension between solvability and challenge.
