DEF-005 · WHAC-A-MOLE

DEF — Deflection Prototype #005 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Moles pop up from holes. Click them to whack them before they disappear. You have 60 seconds.

  • Click — Whack a mole (+100 pts, +200 for golden moles)
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Build combos: Consecutive hits multiply your score. Miss a mole or click an empty hole and the combo resets.
  • Watch for golden moles: They appear rarely but are worth double points.
  • Don't spam click: Clicking empty holes costs you 50 points and breaks your combo.
  • Speed increases: Moles appear faster and stay for less time as the game progresses.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Mogura Taiji (1975, TOGO) → Whac-A-Mole (1975, Aaron Fechter / Creative Engineering) → arcade redemption games worldwide

Aaron Fechter invented the Whac-A-Mole at Creative Engineering Inc. in Orlando, Florida in 1975. Originally a mechanical arcade game with a padded mallet, it became the definitive arcade redemption game — the one you played for tickets. The concept was so intuitive that "whack-a-mole" entered common language as a metaphor for futile, reactive problem-solving.

The Japanese version, Mogura Taiji ("mole extermination"), appeared independently around the same time from TOGO/Bandai, suggesting the mechanic is a convergent design — the game equivalent of natural selection arriving at the same solution twice.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 2–5 (Expert Systems → Statistical)

Contemporary AI: Fitts's Law (1954) formalized the speed-accuracy tradeoff in motor tasks. Later work on selective attention (Broadbent, 1958) and the cocktail party problem maps directly to Whac-A-Mole's core challenge.

Hidden Intelligence: This game is a near-perfect Fitts's Law apparatus. The target (mole) has a fixed size and appears at varying distances from the cursor. As moles appear faster, the game shifts from a simple reaction-time task to an attention-allocation problem: with 9 possible locations and limited time, you must decide where to look. This is the visual search problem that drives modern computer vision research.

Classification

  • Mechanic: Reaction time targeting

Prototype Notes

3×3 grid, 60-second timer, progressive difficulty. Golden moles at ~10% frequency. Combo system rewards accuracy over speed. Miss penalty discourages blind clicking.
