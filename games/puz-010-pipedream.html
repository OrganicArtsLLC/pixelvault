PUZ-010 · PIPE DREAM

PUZ — Puzzle Prototype #010 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Place pipe segments on the grid to build a path for water flowing from the green source. The longer your path, the higher your score.

  • Click a grid cell to place the current pipe piece
  • You can replace pipes that haven't been filled yet
  • Water starts flowing after a countdown — place pipes quickly!
  • Score points for each pipe segment the water fills
  • Game ends when water hits a dead end or the grid edge
  • R — Restart game
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Pipe Types

  • Straight — horizontal or vertical passage
  • Corner — 90-degree bend (4 rotations)
  • Cross — intersection allowing flow in all 4 directions

Tips

  • Plan ahead using the piece queue on the right
  • Cross pieces are valuable — they connect in all directions
  • Try to loop the path back near earlier segments for longer routes
  • Each level speeds up the water flow

History & Lineage

Pipe Mania (1989) was created by The Assembly Line — principally Teoman Irmak and Stephen Grand — and published by Empire Interactive in the UK. LucasArts published it in North America under the name Pipe Dream. The concept — connecting segments under time pressure — proved so compelling it became a universal minigame mechanic, appearing as hacking puzzles in BioShock (2007), bypass minigames in Mass Effect (2007), lockpicking in various RPGs, and flow puzzles in mobile games like Flow Free (2012).

The random piece queue draws directly from Tetris's design philosophy: you can see what's coming but can't choose the order, creating tension between planning and adaptation.

AI Archaeology

Era 3: Knowledge Explosion — Pipe Dream exemplifies online graph construction under uncertainty. The player builds a directed path in a partially observable environment, making irrevocable placement decisions before knowing future pieces. This is the stochastic planning problem: optimal play requires reasoning about probability distributions of upcoming pieces while maintaining connectivity constraints.

From a graph theory perspective, the grid is an implicit graph where pipe placement creates edges. The flowing water performs a depth-first traversal. An AI solver would model this as a Markov decision process, balancing immediate connectivity with future path flexibility.
