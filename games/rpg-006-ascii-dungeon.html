RPG — Role-Playing Prototype #006 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Descend through procedurally generated dungeon levels. Fight monsters, collect items, manage hunger, and try to reach dungeon level 10. Every action takes one turn. Death is permanent.

  • Arrows / WASD — Move (bump into monsters to attack)
  • Space — Wait one turn
  • G — Pick up item
  • I — Open inventory
  • D — Drop item
  • < > — Use stairs
  • E — Eat food from inventory
  • Q — Quaff (drink) potion from inventory
  • R — Restart after death
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Rogue (1980) → Hack (1985) → NetHack (1987) → Dungeon Crawl Stone Soup, Caves of Qud, etc.

NetHack (1987) was developed collaboratively over the nascent internet by the NetHack Dev Team. It built on Hack (1985) and ultimately Rogue (1980), the game that gave "roguelike" its name. Its defining feature was an astonishing depth of item interactions — you could dip a longsword in a potion of holy water, write in the dust with a wand, or polymorph into a dragon. Thousands of interactions emerged from a relatively small rule set.

NetHack popularized ASCII-rendered dungeons with procedural generation, permadeath, and turn-based tactical combat. Its influence on modern game design is immeasurable, from Spelunky to Hades to Dwarf Fortress.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (1980-1993)

Contemporary AI: Expert systems, procedural generation algorithms, early compositional AI research.

Hidden Intelligence: NetHack's item interaction system is a combinatorial rule engine. Each item has properties, and interactions are defined by property combinations. From a small set of rules, an enormous space of emergent behaviors arises — the same principle underlying compositional generalization in modern AI. A potion is not just "healing"; it has physical properties (liquid, throwable, breakable) that interact with the world systematically.

The Convergence: NetHack became a benchmark for AI research. The NetHack Learning Environment (NLE, 2020) challenged RL agents to play NetHack — a task that remains unsolved because it demands long-horizon planning, combinatorial reasoning, and generalization from sparse rewards. It exposed the gap between pattern-matching AI and true reasoning.

If AI Had Designed This: A generative model would create item interactions by learning latent physical properties and composing them — discovering that "fire + scroll = ash" without explicit programming. The dungeon generator would use wave function collapse or constraint satisfaction for more naturalistic layouts.

Classification

Series: RPG — Role-Playing

NetHack is the quintessential roguelike RPG: turn-based, procedurally generated, permadeath, deep systems. It emphasizes knowledge and planning over reflexes.

Core Mechanic

Turn-based dungeon crawl with procedural generation and combinatorial item systems. Every action costs a turn. Monsters act after you. Hunger creates urgency. Permadeath creates stakes. Procedural levels ensure no two runs are identical.

What This Prototype Tests

  • Procedural dungeon generation — do rooms and corridors feel explorable?
  • Turn-based pacing — does the one-action-per-turn rhythm create tactical decisions?
  • Permadeath tension — does permanent death make choices feel consequential?
  • Item interaction — do equipment and consumables create meaningful choices?
  • Hunger pressure — does the food clock prevent infinite cautious play?
