PHY-009 · GOOBRIDGE

PHY — Physics Prototype #009 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Build structures with goo balls to reach the exit pipe! Pick up free goo balls and attach them to the structure to bridge gaps and build towers.

  • Click near a free goo ball — Pick it up
  • Drag — Position the goo ball
  • Release — Attach to nearby structure goo balls
  • R — Restart current level
  • N — Next level (after winning)
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Triangles: Triangle shapes are the strongest structures. Build in triangles!
  • Balance: The structure sways under gravity — distribute weight evenly.
  • Save goo: Unattached goo balls near the pipe get sucked in for bonus score.
  • Wind levels: Watch for wind indicators — build wider bases for stability.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Bridge Builder (2000, Chronic Logic) → Tower of Goo (2005, student project) → World of Goo (2008, 2D Boy)

Kyle Gabler created Tower of Goo as an experimental gameplay project while at Carnegie Mellon. He and Ron Carmel founded 2D Boy and expanded the concept into World of Goo (2008), one of the most acclaimed indie games of its era. The game married structural engineering with character — the goo balls have eyes, make sounds, and feel alive, transforming cold physics into warm emotional play.

The bridge-building genre traces back to Pontifex/Bridge Builder (2000) by Chronic Logic, but World of Goo replaced rigid engineering with organic, squishy construction that anyone could understand intuitively.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 5–6 — Ensemble Methods to Deep Learning (2006–present)

Contemporary AI: Physics engines (Box2D), graph neural networks, structural prediction models.

Hidden Intelligence: Every goo structure is a graph where nodes are balls and edges are springs. Structural stability depends on the graph’s topology — the same math that powers graph neural networks. The game implicitly teaches finite element analysis: distribute forces across a mesh to prevent collapse.

The Convergence: Modern AI uses graph neural networks to predict physical system behavior from connectivity patterns. DeepMind’s “Learning to Simulate” work predicts particle-based physics by reasoning about neighbor relationships — exactly what a World of Goo player does intuitively when deciding where to place the next ball.

Genre: Physics Puzzle / Construction

Prototype Notes

Spring-joint physics with Verlet integration. Goo balls have eyes that track the mouse cursor. Structure detection via flood-fill graph traversal. Four levels with increasing difficulty: simple bridge, vertical tower, bridge with wind, long span. Pipe suction zone collects free goo balls for scoring.
