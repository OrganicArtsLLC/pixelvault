ANC-021 · TOGYZKUMALAK

How to Play

A traditional Kazakh mancala game played on a 2×9 board with 162 stones. Sow stones counterclockwise, capture by landing on exactly 3 in an opponent's pit, and claim a permanent tuzdyk.

  • Click a pit on your side (bottom row) to sow
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Rules

  • Setup: 9 pits per side, 9 stones each (162 total). Two kazans (score pits).
  • Sowing: Pick up all stones from a pit, drop one in each subsequent pit counterclockwise. If a pit has exactly 1 stone, move only that stone to the next pit.
  • Capture: If your last stone lands in an opponent's pit making it exactly 3, those 3 stones go to your kazan.
  • Tuzdyk: When you capture exactly 3 from an opponent's pit, that pit becomes your tuzdyk — permanently claimed. All future stones landing there go directly to your kazan. Each player may have only one tuzdyk. You cannot tuzdyk the opponent's 9th pit (rightmost).
  • Win: Collect 82 or more stones in your kazan. If both reach 81, it is a draw.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Mancala/Oware (~700 CE) → Togyzkumalak (Central Asian nomadic peoples, traditional)

Togyzkumalak ("nine pebbles") is the national intellectual game of Kazakhstan, played across Central Asia by Kazakh, Kyrgyz, and other Turkic peoples for centuries. It was inscribed on UNESCO's Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2020.

The game was traditionally played by nomadic herders using pebbles and holes dug in the ground. The number 9 holds cultural significance — nine is sacred in Turkic numerology. The 2×9 board with 162 stones creates far deeper positional play than the Western Kalah variant.

The tuzdyk mechanic — permanently claiming an opponent's pit — has no parallel in any other mancala family game. It creates an irreversible topological change to the board that fundamentally alters the flow of stones for the remainder of the game.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 2 — Expert Systems (heuristic rules, knowledge encoding)

Contemporary AI: The tuzdyk decision encodes a meta-strategic choice: when to permanently alter the board topology versus when to accumulate stones through normal capture. This is a heuristic reasoning problem — weighing irreversible structural advantage against immediate material gain.

Hidden Intelligence: The tuzdyk transforms the game from pure accumulation into territory control. A well-placed tuzdyk can redirect stone flow for dozens of subsequent moves, creating a positional advantage that compounds over time. The constraint that each player gets only one tuzdyk makes the timing decision critical.

The Convergence: The permanent pit capture mechanic anticipates concepts in modern game AI: irreversible state changes, topology modification, and the tension between exploitation (capture now) and structural advantage (alter the board permanently).

If AI Had Designed This: An optimization algorithm exploring mancala variants would likely discover that adding a single permanent capture rule to a larger board (2×9 vs 2×6) dramatically increases strategic depth while adding minimal rule complexity — exactly what Togyzkumalak achieves.
