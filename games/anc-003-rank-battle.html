ANC-003 · RANK BATTLE

ANC — Ancient Games Prototype #003 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Place your pieces, then move them to attack and capture the enemy Flag. Piece ranks are hidden until combat.

  • Setup: Click a piece in the tray, then click a cell in rows 5-6 to place it. Click an already-placed piece to swap.
  • Auto-place: Click "AUTO" to randomly place remaining pieces.
  • Play: Click your piece to select, then click a valid destination (green dots) to move.
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Ranks (lower number = stronger)

  • 1 Marshal — Strongest piece (killed by Spy if Spy attacks)
  • 2 General — Second strongest
  • 3 Colonel, 4 Major, 5 Captain, 6 Lieutenant, 7 Sergeant
  • 8 Miner — Only piece that can defuse Bombs
  • 9 Scout — Moves any number of squares in a line
  • S Spy — Kills Marshal if Spy attacks; loses to everything else
  • B Bomb — Cannot move; destroys attackers (except Miner)
  • F Flag — Cannot move; capture it to win!

Combat

  • Lower rank number wins (Marshal 1 beats General 2, etc.)
  • Equal ranks: both pieces are destroyed
  • Spy attacking Marshal: Spy wins
  • Any piece attacking Bomb: attacker dies (unless Miner)
  • Miner attacking Bomb: Bomb is defused, Miner survives

History & Lineage

Ancestry: L'Attaque (1909) → Stratego (1947, Mogendorff) → Milton Bradley (1961)

Stratego descends from L'Attaque, a French game patented in 1909. Jacques Johan Mogendorff acquired and refined the design, publishing it in the Netherlands in 1947. Milton Bradley brought it to the US in 1961, where it became one of the best-selling board games of the 20th century.

The genius of Stratego is hidden information. Unlike chess, you don't know what you're attacking until contact. Every move is a question: is that piece guarding the flag, or is it bait? The game is as much about reading your opponent as calculating positions.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 2 — Expert Systems (1965-1985)

Contemporary AI: Stratego's hidden information makes it fundamentally harder than chess for AI. You can't minimax what you can't see. Instead, the game demands probabilistic reasoning — maintaining beliefs about opponent piece identities and updating them with each observation.

Hidden Intelligence: Each combat reveals one data point, but the absence of movement reveals even more. A piece that never moves is likely a Bomb or Flag. A piece that charges forward is probably expendable. The game is a natural laboratory for Bayesian inference in adversarial settings.

The Convergence: DeepNash (2022, DeepMind) achieved expert-level Stratego play using game-theoretic reasoning without search — a breakthrough because traditional game tree methods fail with hidden information. Stratego was one of the last classic board games to fall to AI.

If AI Had Designed This: It might have added more information channels — sound, weight, partial reveals — to increase the inference surface area and make deduction richer.

Core Mechanic

Hidden ranks, bluff and deduction, capture by rank comparison. Imperfect information creates a deduction layer atop tactical movement. Every engagement is an intelligence operation.
