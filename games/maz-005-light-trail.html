MAZ-005 · LIGHT TRAIL

MAZ — Maze Prototype #005 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Pilot your light cycle across the grid. You leave a permanent wall trail behind you. Your opponent does the same. Crash into any wall or trail and you lose the round. Last cycle standing wins.

Tips

  • Think ahead: Every move shrinks the free space. Plan your path to keep options open.
  • Trap the AI: Try to cut off the opponent into a smaller region so it runs out of room first.
  • Use the walls: Hugging the border can extend your survival but limits escape routes.
  • Speed increases: Each round gets slightly faster. Adapt your strategy.
  • Best of 5: First to 3 round wins takes the match.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Blockade (1976, Gremlin) → Snafu (1978, Mattel) → Tron (1982, Bally Midway)

Blockade (1976) by Gremlin Industries was the first light-trail game — two players leaving walls in their wake on a grid, trying to survive longer than the opponent. It was a pure territory-denial duel stripped to its simplest possible form.

Mattel's Snafu (1978) brought the concept to the Intellivision console with multiple speed settings and AI opponents. But it was the Tron arcade cabinet (1982, Bally Midway) that immortalized the mechanic, tying it to Disney's groundbreaking film. The Light Cycles sub-game was only one of four mini-games in the cabinet, but it became the most iconic — the neon trails on black void became a visual shorthand for digital combat.

The mechanic has proven timeless: from Nibbles (QBasic, 1991) to Tron: Evolution (2010) to countless browser games. The core tension — every move simultaneously creates and destroys possibility — never gets old.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (1978–1983)

Contemporary AI: Expert systems reaching commercial viability. Early neural network research reviving. Game AI primarily rule-based with simple heuristics.

Hidden Intelligence: Light cycle games create a pure adversarial territory-denial problem. The shrinking free space is a constraint satisfaction problem that becomes harder each frame — prefiguring the bounded-resource reasoning central to real-time AI planning. Each move irrevocably commits resources (space), making rollback impossible.

The Convergence: The game is equivalent to a two-player graph-cutting problem on a grid. Modern approaches use Voronoi partitioning to estimate territory control and flood-fill to count reachable cells — techniques that connect to computational geometry and network flow algorithms. The AI must balance survival (avoiding dead ends) with aggression (cutting off the opponent).

If AI Had Designed This: Adaptive difficulty through Voronoi-based territory analysis — the AI would dynamically adjust its aggression based on the ratio of reachable cells between the two players, creating perfectly balanced matches that always feel close.

Classification

Series: MAZ — Maze

Games built around navigation of constrained space. In Tron, the maze is not pre-built but dynamically created by both players — you are simultaneously the navigator and the maze architect.

Core Mechanic

Territory denial through permanent trail walls. Grid-based movement at constant speed. Two-player adversarial (human vs AI). No power-ups, no weapons — pure spatial reasoning. Best-of-5 match structure with escalating speed.
