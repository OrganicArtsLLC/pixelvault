How to Play

Command your blue army against the red AI in turn-based grid combat. Move units, attack enemies, capture towns, and destroy the enemy HQ or all their units to win.

  • Click a friendly unit to select it
  • Click a highlighted tile to move the selected unit
  • Click an enemy in attack range (red highlight) to attack
  • Enter — End your turn
  • R — Restart game
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Units

  • Infantry — 2 move, 3 attack, 5 HP. Reliable all-rounder.
  • Tank — 3 move, 5 attack, 8 HP. Powerful but costs 15 gold.
  • Sniper — 1 move, 4 attack at range 3-4, 3 HP. Fragile but deadly from afar.
  • Medic — 2 move, heals adjacent allies 2 HP, 3 HP. Keep behind the front line.

Terrain

  • Plains — Normal movement, no bonus.
  • Forest — +1 defense, costs 2 movement points.
  • Mountain — Impassable.
  • Town — +2 defense. Move a unit onto it to capture. Generates 5 gold/turn.
  • HQ — Your base. Buy units here (1-4 keys). Capture enemy HQ to win.

Tips

  • Capture towns early for income advantage.
  • Use forests for defensive positions — the +1 defense adds up.
  • Snipers are devastating but fragile — protect them with infantry.
  • Medics can turn a close fight by keeping your tanks alive.
  • Focus fire on one enemy unit at a time rather than spreading damage.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Battle Isle (1991, Blue Byte) → Advance Wars (2001, Intelligent Systems) → Great Little War Game (2011, Rubicon)

Turn-based tactics games trace back to tabletop wargames like Kriegsspiel (1812). The digital lineage runs through SSI's hex wargames of the 1980s, Nintendo Wars (Famicom Wars, 1988), and its masterful successor Advance Wars, which proved that deep tactical gameplay could be wrapped in an accessible, colorful package.

Great Little War Game (2011) brought this formula to mobile with charming 3D visuals and streamlined controls, demonstrating that the core move-attack-capture loop remains compelling across platforms and generations.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 4 — Statistical Learning (1990-2000)

The AI in tactics games typically uses evaluation functions that score board positions based on unit health, positioning, terrain advantage, and territory control. This connects to classical minimax search with alpha-beta pruning, augmented by heuristic evaluation functions — the same approach that powered early chess engines.

Modern tactics AI often uses influence maps to evaluate territorial control, identifying chokepoints and vulnerable flanks. The discrete nature of the grid makes exhaustive move enumeration feasible, allowing deeper tactical reasoning than continuous-space games.

Prototype Notes

This prototype captures the essential tactics loop: position units, exploit terrain, manage resources, and outmaneuver the opponent. The AI evaluates threats, captures towns, focuses fire on weakened units, and attempts flanking maneuvers. Unit variety (infantry/tank/sniper/medic) creates rock-paper-scissors dynamics without explicit counters — it's about positioning and timing.
