SIM-014 · COLONY TRADE

SIM — Simulation Prototype #014 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Colonize the planet Irata! Claim land, equip M.U.L.E.s (Multiple Use Labor Elements) to produce resources, and trade in real-time auctions. Highest net worth after 6 rounds wins.

  • Click a plot during Land Grant to claim it
  • Click Food / Energy / Ore buttons to equip your M.U.L.E.
  • During Auction: Click & hold to raise/lower your price
  • Mouse up/down in auction to adjust price
  • R — Restart game
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Resources

  • Food — needed each round; shortages cost time. Plains produce most.
  • Energy — powers your M.U.L.E.s; without it, production halves. Mountains produce most.
  • Ore — valuable export; mountains yield the most ore.

Auction

  • Sellers start at bottom, buyers at top of the price bar
  • Move toward each other — trade happens when you meet
  • Supply and demand set the range; strategy sets the final price

History

M.U.L.E. (1983) was designed by Dani Bunten Berry for Ozark Softscape and published by Electronic Arts. It is widely considered one of the greatest multiplayer games ever made, pioneering cooperative-competitive economic gameplay. The real-time auction mechanic — where players physically walk toward each other to negotiate price — remains one of the most elegant trading systems in game history. Bunten Berry was a visionary designer who believed games should bring people together, and M.U.L.E. embodies that philosophy perfectly.

AI Archaeology

The AI opponents use simple heuristic strategies: they balance resource production, assess supply shortages, and bid in auctions based on need and available cash. The original game's AI was remarkably sophisticated for 1983, creating the illusion of intelligent economic actors through carefully tuned expert-system rules.

Prototype Notes

This prototype captures the core M.U.L.E. loop: claim land, equip mules, harvest, auction. The map is simplified to a 9x5 grid with varied terrain. Three AI players use different strategies (balanced, food-focused, ore-focused) to create varied auction dynamics. The auction mechanic preserves the original's buyer-walks-down, seller-walks-up interaction.
