How to Play

Remove all 144 tiles by matching free pairs. A tile is free if nothing sits on top of it and at least one side (left or right) is open.

  • Click a free tile to select it (highlighted in gold).
  • Click a second matching free tile to remove both.
  • Seasons (S1–S4) match any other Season. Flowers (F1–F4) match any other Flower.
  • H — Show a hint (flashes a valid pair)
  • R — Restart with a new shuffle
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Mahjong (~1870, China) → Computer Mahjong Solitaire (1981, Brodie Lockard) → Shanghai (1986, Activision)

Traditional Mahjong is a four-player rummy-style tile game originating in mid-19th century China. The computer solitaire variant was created by Brodie Lockard, a University of Illinois student paralyzed in a gymnastics accident, who programmed the first version on the PLATO system in 1981 using a mouth-stick to type. The PLATO version spread across university networks before reaching commercial audiences.

Shanghai (1986): Activision licensed the concept and released it as "Shanghai," making Mahjong Solitaire a mainstream hit and one of the best-selling puzzle games of the late 1980s. It established the turtle/pyramid layout as the canonical arrangement and spawned countless imitators. The game was particularly popular on early Macintosh computers.

Cultural Impact: Mahjong Solitaire remains one of the most-played casual games worldwide, pre-installed on millions of PCs. It introduced many Western players to Mahjong tile iconography without any connection to the original four-player game.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 2 — Expert Systems (pattern matching, constrained search)

The Visibility-Action Gap: Mahjong Solitaire creates a fascinating cognitive puzzle: you can see tiles you cannot act on. Unlike hidden-information games, the constraint is structural — a tile is visible but blocked. This maps directly to constraint satisfaction problems in AI.

Search Complexity: The branching factor varies wildly during play. Early moves may have 20+ valid pairs; late game often narrows to 1-2. A greedy strategy (always match the first pair found) leads to dead ends roughly 10% of the time even on solvable layouts. Optimal play requires look-ahead search to avoid creating unsolvable board states.

Solvability: Not all shuffles are solvable. Research shows that roughly 97-99% of random turtle layouts are solvable with perfect play, but finding the solution requires backtracking search — a classic AI technique from the expert systems era.

Prototype Notes

This prototype implements the classic turtle layout with 144 tiles (36 types × 4 copies). Tiles are rendered with a 2.5D stacked effect using offset shadows. The hint system finds valid pairs using the same freedom-check logic the player must internalize. Seasons and Flowers follow traditional Mahjong matching rules (any season matches any season).
