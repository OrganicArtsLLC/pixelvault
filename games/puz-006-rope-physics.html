PUZ-006 · ROPE PHYSICS

PUZ — Puzzle Prototype #006 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Cut ropes by clicking and dragging across them to release the candy. Guide it into the monster's mouth to complete each level!

  • Mouse drag — Cut ropes (click and drag across a rope)
  • R — Restart current level
  • N — Next level (after completing current)
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Stars: Swing the candy through stars before feeding the monster for bonus points. Each level has 3 stars.
  • Air cushions: Green fans blow the candy sideways. Time your cuts to ride the wind.
  • Bubbles: Blue bubbles float the candy upward. Tap the bubble to pop it and release.
  • Multiple ropes: Later levels have multiple ropes. The order you cut them matters!
  • Momentum: Let the candy swing to build momentum before cutting.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Cut the Rope (2010, ZeptoLab) → The Incredible Machine (1993, Sierra) → World of Goo (2008, 2D Boy)

Cut the Rope was created by Russian brothers Semyon and Efim Voskresenskiy under their studio ZeptoLab in 2010. It became one of the first mega-hits of the touchscreen era, demonstrating that physics puzzles designed natively for touch could rival the depth and satisfaction of traditional desktop games.

The game distilled complex rope physics into a single intuitive gesture: the swipe to cut. Its character Om Nom became one of mobile gaming's most recognizable mascots. The design lineage traces back through World of Goo's structural physics puzzles and The Incredible Machine's Rube Goldberg contraptions.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 5 — Deep Learning (2012-present)

Contemporary AI: Physics simulation engines, neural network approaches to physical reasoning. DeepMind's work on learning physics from observation.

Cut the Rope's puzzle design is essentially a constrained physics optimization problem. Each level asks: given these anchor points, rope lengths, and environmental forces, what sequence of cuts delivers the payload to the target? This maps directly to trajectory optimization problems in robotics and the physical reasoning benchmarks that challenge modern AI systems. The game proved that humans solve these intuitively through mental simulation — the same capability that remains surprisingly difficult for AI.

Prototype Notes

This prototype implements verlet integration for rope physics with 10 levels of progressive difficulty. Elements include multiple ropes, air cushions, and bubbles. The rope cutting uses line-segment intersection for precise detection. Each level is hand-designed with 3 collectible stars rewarding creative solutions beyond the minimum path.
