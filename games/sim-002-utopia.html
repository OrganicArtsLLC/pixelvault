SIM-002 · ISLAND REALM

SIM — Simulation Prototype #002 ★★★★☆

How to Play

Build a thriving island civilization. Place buildings on the grid to produce food, generate gold, and house your population. Reach 100 population to win.

  • 1 — Farm ($10) — produces food to feed your people
  • 2 — House ($15) — increases population capacity by 8
  • 3 — Fort ($25) — defends nearby buildings from pirates
  • 4 — Factory ($30) — generates gold income
  • 5 — Fishing Boat ($20) — placed on water, produces food and gold
  • Mouse Click — Place selected building on the grid
  • Space — Pause / Resume
  • R — Restart
  • ? — Toggle this info panel

Tips

  • Balance resources: Population grows when you have surplus food and housing capacity. No food means starvation.
  • Farms first: Without food, your population will decline. Build farms early.
  • Forts matter: Pirates will raid undefended buildings. Forts protect tiles within a 2-cell radius.
  • Fishing boats: Must be placed on water tiles. They produce both food and gold.
  • Watch for storms: They can damage buildings. Rebuild quickly.

History & Lineage

Ancestry: Utopia (1982, Don Daglow, Intellivision) → SimCity (1989, Maxis) → Civilization (1991, MicroProse)

Utopia (1982) by Don Daglow was arguably the first god game and the first real-time strategy game. Two players managed competing islands on the Intellivision, placing crops, building forts, and sending fishing boats and PT boats. It introduced the concept of real-time resource management as the core gameplay loop.

The game pioneered ideas that would later define the simulation genre: indirect control over population, resource balancing, and the tension between economic growth and military defense. Will Wright cited Utopia as an influence on SimCity.

AI Archaeology

AI Era: 3 — Knowledge Explosion (1980-1987)

Contemporary AI: Expert systems were emerging (MYCIN, DENDRAL). Multi-variable optimization was being formalized. The idea of balancing interconnected systems was central to operations research.

Hidden Intelligence: Utopia's resource management loop — food feeds population, population generates tax revenue, revenue buys buildings that produce more food — is a coupled dynamical system. Each variable depends on others, creating feedback loops. Expert systems of the era were beginning to model exactly these kinds of interdependent decision problems.

The Convergence: Modern city-builder AI uses reinforcement learning to balance the same variables Daglow hand-tuned in 1982. The "build, observe, adjust" loop is essentially a human doing gradient descent on a multi-objective reward function.

If AI Had Designed This: An RL agent would discover the optimal build order — farms first for food security, then houses to grow population, factories only after food surplus is stable. This is the strategy most human players converge on through trial and error.

Classification

Series: SIM — Simulation

Utopia sits at the origin of the god game / city-builder lineage. Real-time resource management with indirect population control.

Core Mechanic

Real-time resource balancing on a grid. Place buildings to produce food and gold. Population grows when food and housing allow. Random events (storms, pirates) threaten your buildings. Reach the population target to win.

What This Prototype Tests

  • Resource interdependence — does the food/gold/population triangle create interesting decisions?
  • Build placement — does spatial layout on the island grid matter?
  • Random events — do storms and pirates create meaningful disruption?
  • Pacing — does the real-time tick rate feel right for strategic decision-making?

Prototype Notes

This is a sketch rated 4/5. Grid-based island with water border. Five building types with resource production. Random events add challenge. Population target win condition.
